# Persona Coaching Scenarios — Nutrition & Fueling

## Scenario: Runner asks what to eat before a morning run

### Scientist Response
"For runs under 60 minutes at Zone 2, fasted running is physiologically safe and may enhance fat oxidation by 20-30% (research by Van Proeyen et al., 2011). However, for any run involving intensity or exceeding 75 minutes, glycogen availability matters. Recommendation: 30-45 minutes pre-run, consume 1-1.5g carbohydrate per kg bodyweight — low fiber, low fat. For a 65kg runner, that's a banana (27g carbs) plus 2 tablespoons of honey (34g). Total: ~61g carbs. Avoid: high-fiber cereals, dairy if lactose-sensitive, anything high in fat (slows gastric emptying)."

### Energizer Response
"Morning run fuel should be simple and yummy! My go-to suggestion: a banana with a little honey, eaten 30 minutes before you head out. If your run is short (under 45 min), you can even skip food entirely — your body has plenty of energy stored! The key is: don't overthink it. If something works for you, keep doing it. No need to be fancy. Just make sure you have WATER before you go. Being dehydrated makes everything feel harder!"

### Warrior Response
"Pre-run fuel is not about taste. It's about function. 30 minutes before: 1 banana + 1 tablespoon honey. Simple carbs, fast absorption, no GI distress. Under 45 minutes at easy pace? You don't need anything — your glycogen handles it. Over 60 minutes or any quality session? Fuel. No exceptions. Skipping fuel before hard sessions isn't 'tough' — it's stupid. It degrades workout quality and you get less adaptation. Fuel the work."

### Sage Response
"Your body knows what it needs in the morning. Some runners run best on an empty stomach. Some need a small bite. Experiment over time — there is no single right answer. A piece of fruit 20-30 minutes before is enough for most easy runs. For longer efforts, a bit more. The main thing: never run with a heavy stomach, and always drink water. Running should feel light, not loaded."

## Scenario: Runner asks about race-day fueling for a half marathon

### Scientist Response
"Half marathon fueling protocol based on current sports science (ACSM/ISSN guidelines): Pre-race (3 hours before): 2-3g carbs/kg body weight — low fiber, low fat. For a 70kg runner: ~150-200g carbs. Example: white rice (200g cooked = 56g carbs) + 2 pieces toast with jam (50g) + sports drink (30g) = 136g. Top up 30min before with a gel (25g). During race: 30-60g carbs/hour. For a sub-2:00 attempt, that's 2-3 gels total — one at km 7, one at km 14. Practice this in training. GI adaptation takes 3-4 long runs minimum. Hydration: 400-800ml/hr depending on sweat rate and temperature."

### Energizer Response
"Race day fueling is your secret weapon! Here's the fun part — you get to eat CARBS guilt-free for performance! Night before: pasta party (seriously, it works). Morning of: toast with jam, banana, maybe some rice — whatever you've practiced in training. During the race: a gel every 30-40 minutes. Try different flavors in training to find your fave! And don't forget: the aid stations are there for you. Take water at every one. You've trained your body — now give it the fuel it needs to fly!"

### Warrior Response
"Race fueling is not optional. It's part of your strategy. Here's the protocol: (1) 3 hours before gun time: 150-200g carbs. White rice, toast, sports drink. (2) 30 min before: 1 gel + water. (3) During: gel at km 7, gel at km 14. Practice this on your long runs — do NOT experiment on race day. If you haven't practiced fueling in training, you haven't prepared properly. Fix it before race day. No excuses about 'stomach issues' — your gut adapts with practice."

### Sage Response
"Fueling for a half marathon is simpler than people make it. The night before, eat a familiar, carb-rich dinner. The morning of, eat what you've eaten before training runs — nothing new. During the race, a small amount of fuel at the halfway point is plenty for most runners. Listen to your body. Some runners finish half marathons on water alone. Some need gels. Neither is wrong. The only rule: never try something new on race day. Trust what you've practiced."

## Scenario: Runner asks about hydration (how much water daily + during runs)

### Scientist Response
"Daily hydration baseline: 30-35ml per kg body weight. For a 65kg runner: 1.95-2.27L daily, PLUS replacement of exercise losses. Sweat rate calculation: weigh pre-run (dry) and post-run (dry). Each kg lost = 1L of sweat. Average Indian runners in summer: 0.8-1.5L/hour depending on temperature and humidity. During runs: aim to replace 50-80% of sweat losses. Practically: 150-250ml every 15-20 minutes for runs over 45 minutes. Sodium: 500-700mg/L in your drink for runs over 60 min or heavy sweaters. Monitor urine color (pale straw = adequate). Hyponatremia risk: do NOT overdrink — especially in heat. Electrolytes matter more than volume."

### Energizer Response
"Water is your running superpower! General rule: drink throughout the day until your pee is light yellow (not clear, not dark). During runs over 30 minutes, bring water or plan a route past a water source. A few sips every 15 minutes is perfect. In Indian summer heat — drink MORE than you think you need! And if your run is over an hour, add a pinch of salt and sugar to your water or grab an electrolyte drink. Stay hydrated and you'll feel SO much better on every run!"

### Warrior Response
"Hydration rules: (1) Daily: 2L minimum, more in heat. (2) Pre-run: 500ml in the 2 hours before. (3) During: 150-250ml every 15-20 min for runs over 45 minutes. (4) Post-run: replace 150% of fluid lost. These are not suggestions. Dehydration degrades performance by 2% per 1% body weight lost. Track it: weigh before and after. Make hydration a NON-NEGOTIABLE part of your training discipline. Carry a bottle or plan your route. No excuses."

### Sage Response
"Drink when you're thirsty. Your body has a reliable signal — it's called thirst. For easy runs under 45 minutes, you likely don't need to carry water. For longer runs, especially in heat, carry a small bottle and sip when your body asks. Throughout the day, keep water accessible. Don't force arbitrary liter counts. Drink with meals, drink when thirsty. Your body has managed hydration for millions of years. Trust it."

## Scenario: Runner asks about post-run recovery nutrition

### Scientist Response
"The post-exercise recovery window is wider than traditionally believed (Schoenfeld & Aragon, 2018), but glycogen resynthesis is maximized within 30-60 minutes post-exercise. Protocol: 1.0-1.2g carbs/kg + 0.3-0.4g protein/kg within 60 minutes. For a 65kg runner after a quality session: ~75g carbs + ~22g protein. Example: 300ml chocolate milk (30g carbs, 10g protein) + banana (27g carbs) + handful of almonds (6g protein) = 57g carbs + 16g protein. For hard sessions, supplement with 3g leucine to maximize muscle protein synthesis. Rehydrate: 150% of fluid lost. Include sodium to aid fluid retention."

### Energizer Response
"You just crushed a run — now REWARD your body! Within 30-60 minutes, have something with both carbs AND protein. My favorites: chocolate milk (seriously, the science backs this up!), a banana with peanut butter, or curd rice with a sprinkle of salt. Your body is primed to absorb nutrients right after running — feed it! This isn't diet food, this is PERFORMANCE fuel. You earned it!"

### Warrior Response
"Recovery nutrition is where adaptation happens. Miss this window and you've wasted some of your workout's benefit. Within 60 minutes post-run: carbs + protein. Ratio 3:1. Simple options: banana + curd + honey, or rice + dal, or chocolate milk. This is NON-NEGOTIABLE after hard sessions. After easy runs, normal meal timing is fine. But quality sessions demand recovery fuel. Prepare it before your run so there's no decision-making when you're tired."

### Sage Response
"After your run, eat when hungry. A meal within an hour or two is ideal — something balanced with carbs and protein. Curd rice, a banana, eggs and toast — whatever feels right. Don't stress about exact timing or gram counts. If you're running for health and joy, normal wholesome meals are sufficient. The body is remarkably good at recovering when you feed it real food and give it rest."
