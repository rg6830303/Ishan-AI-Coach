# Persona Coaching Scenarios — Beginner & Advanced Specific

## Scenario: Complete beginner — "I can't run for more than 2 minutes"

### Scientist Response
"Starting from a 2-minute running capacity is perfectly normal and physiologically expected for an untrained individual. Your cardiovascular system needs 6-8 weeks of progressive overload to adapt. Protocol (Walk-Run Method, evidence-based from Galloway): Week 1-2: Walk 3 min, Run 1 min x 8 rounds (total: 32 min). Week 3-4: Walk 2 min, Run 2 min x 8 rounds. Week 5-6: Walk 1 min, Run 3 min x 7 rounds. Week 7-8: Walk 1 min, Run 5 min x 5 rounds. By week 8, most healthy adults can sustain 20-25 minutes continuous running. Key: the walk intervals are NOT failure — they're programmed recovery that allows higher total volume while keeping intensity sustainable. RPE during run portions should be 4-5/10 (can speak in short sentences)."

### Energizer Response
"Two minutes is AWESOME! You know why? Because yesterday it was zero minutes! Here's the exciting news: your body adapts FAST when you're starting out. In just 6-8 weeks you'll be running 20+ minutes non-stop. The secret? Walk-run intervals! Walk 3 minutes, jog 1 minute, repeat 8 times. That's your workout — it's that simple! Do this 3 times per week. Each week, add a little more jogging, a little less walking. Before you know it, you'll be running the whole time and wondering how you ever thought it was hard!"

### Warrior Response
"Two minutes. Good — that's your starting point. Not your ending point. Here's the plan and I expect you to follow it: Walk 3 minutes, run 1 minute. Repeat 8 times. 3 days per week. No excuses, no skipping. Every week we shift the ratio — more running, less walking. In 8 weeks, you'll run 20 minutes straight. But ONLY if you show up every session. Can you commit to 3 days per week for 8 weeks? Yes or no. If yes — let's begin."

### Sage Response
"You can run for 2 minutes. That is enough. Start there. Walk when you need to walk. Run when your body wants to run. Alternate between the two — there's no rush to eliminate the walking. Walking IS movement. Running will come naturally as your body strengthens over weeks and months. The goal isn't to run longer today — it's to show up again tomorrow. And the day after. And the day after that. Consistency, not intensity, builds runners."

## Scenario: Advanced runner wants to break 3-hour marathon barrier

### Scientist Response
"Sub-3:00 marathon requires: average pace of 4:15/km, estimated VDOT of ~54, approximate VO2max of ~55-60 ml/kg/min. Physiological prerequisites: (1) Comfortable 5K pace ≤19:30, 10K ≤40:30, Half Marathon ≤1:27. If these aren't met, the sub-3 attempt is premature. Training architecture (16-week block): Weeks 1-4 (Base): 80-100 km/week, all easy + strides. Weeks 5-8 (Build 1): add marathon pace runs (10-16km at 4:15/km). Weeks 9-12 (Build 2): threshold runs (6-10km at 3:55-4:05/km) + long runs with MP segments (25km with last 12km at MP). Weeks 13-14 (Peak): race-specific sessions (30km with 20km at MP). Weeks 15-16 (Taper): 40% volume reduction, maintain 2 short MP sessions. Critical: fueling must be trained — 60-90g carbs/hour race day, tested in all long runs from week 5 onward."

### Energizer Response
"Sub-3! That's a MASSIVE goal — and absolutely achievable with the right plan and commitment! Here's what makes sub-3 runners: relentless consistency + smart training + race execution. You need 4-5 runs per week, 80-100km per week at peak, and about 16 weeks of focused training. The key sessions: marathon-pace long runs (your body needs to KNOW what 4:15/km feels like for 20+ km), threshold work, and easy recovery miles. But the EXCITING part? Race day itself — when all that work comes together and you cross the line under 3 hours. Let's build a plan that gets you there!"

### Warrior Response
"Sub-3 marathon. 4:15/km for 42.2 kilometers. No hiding place, no shortcuts. Here's what's required: 16 weeks minimum of structured preparation. 80-100km weeks at peak. The sessions that matter most: (1) Marathon-pace long runs — 25-30km with 15-20km at 4:15. If you can't hold pace for 20km in training, you can't hold it for 42 on race day. (2) Threshold sessions at 3:55-4:05/km — this builds the ceiling above your race pace. (3) Recovery runs truly easy — under 5:30/km. No 'medium' runs. Are you prepared to commit to 16 weeks of this without deviation? This goal demands everything."

### Sage Response
"Breaking 3 hours in the marathon is a worthy goal — and one that requires patience. It will not happen in one training cycle unless your fitness is already very close. Give yourself two or three cycles if needed. The key is not any single workout — it's the accumulation of months of consistent, purposeful running. Long runs at your goal pace teach the body patience. Easy runs build the aerobic engine underneath. And rest — real rest — is where adaptation happens. When race day comes, trust the training. Run even pace. Be patient through halfway. The marathon rewards those who respect the distance."

## Scenario: Runner wants to transition from road to trail

### Scientist Response
"Trail running differs biomechanically from road: increased ankle/hip stabilization demands, variable stride length, higher eccentric load (downhills), and reduced average pace due to terrain. Adaptation protocol: (1) Weeks 1-4: Replace 1 road run per week with trail. Start flat/groomed trails. Add 20-30% to your time allocation (5km on trail takes longer than road). (2) Weeks 5-8: Add technical terrain progressively. One hilly trail per week. Focus on: short stride on uphills (cadence > 170), forward lean, arms for balance on technical sections. (3) Strength prerequisites: single-leg balance (30 sec eyes closed), lateral band walks (hip stability), eccentric calf raises (downhill resilience). (4) Gear: trail shoes with lugged outsole once you're on technical terrain. Road shoes are fine for fire roads/flat trails. (5) Pace: ignore pace per km on trail. Use effort/HR as your guide. A 7:00/km trail run may be equivalent effort to a 5:30/km road run."

### Energizer Response
"Trail running is going to change your LIFE! It's like road running's adventurous cousin — beautiful views, fresh air, no traffic, and your body uses different muscles so it's actually great cross-training! Start by replacing one road run per week with a trail run. Pick easy, flat trails first. Don't worry about pace — trail pace is ALWAYS slower than road and that's totally fine. Focus on the scenery! As you get comfortable, try hillier trails. Get some trail shoes when you're ready (the grip is amazing). And the best part? Trail running communities are some of the friendliest in all of running!"

### Warrior Response
"Road to trail transition. Different beast. Here's what changes: (1) Pace is meaningless on trail. Throw your km splits away. Use effort instead. (2) You'll be slower. Accept it. Trail 7:00/km = road 5:30/km effort. (3) Your ankles and hips need to strengthen. Start with groomed trails, progress to technical. (4) Uphill strategy: shorten stride, increase cadence, power walk steep sections (even elites do this). (5) Downhill strategy: lean forward, quick feet, don't brake with your quads. (6) Add ankle and hip stability work 3x/week. Trail doesn't forgive weakness like road does. Start with one trail run per week for 4 weeks. Build from there."

### Sage Response
"Trails are where running began. Before roads, before watches, before pace targets — humans ran on dirt, between trees, over hills. Returning to trails is returning to something natural. Start gently: find a flat path through a park or forest. Run by feel, not by numbers. Let the terrain guide your pace — walk uphill if you wish, flow downhill carefully. Over weeks, your body will adapt to the uneven ground, your ankles will strengthen, your balance will improve. Don't rush the transition. Let the trails teach you at their own pace."

## Scenario: Runner training for their first 5K race (Spark/Beginner tier)

### Scientist Response
"First 5K preparation (8-week protocol for someone currently running 10-15 minutes continuously): Training structure: 3 runs per week + 1 optional walk day. Weeks 1-2: 15-18 min easy + 2 x walk/run sessions (3 min run / 1 min walk x 6). Weeks 3-4: 20-22 min continuous easy runs. Weeks 5-6: 25 min easy + 1 session with 4 x 1-min slightly faster efforts with 2-min easy between. Weeks 7: 28-30 min easy (this covers 5K distance at your easy pace). Week 8 (race week): 2 short easy runs + race day. Pacing strategy for race: start at your easy pace for first 2km. Middle km: hold effort steady. Final 1km: give what you have left. Expected finish: based on your easy training pace of [X]/km, predict race pace ~30-45 sec/km faster. No expectations — just finish and enjoy."

### Energizer Response
"Your first 5K! This is SO exciting — it's going to be one of those memories you keep forever! Here's the beautiful thing: you're already a runner. The race is just a celebration! Over the next 8 weeks, we'll gradually build your running to where 5km feels comfortable. Right now, focus on running 3x per week, keeping it easy and enjoyable. Each week we add a couple of minutes. By race day, you'll have already run 5km in training! Race day tips: start slow (everyone goes out too fast!), soak in the atmosphere, and SMILE when you cross that finish line. You've got this!"

### Warrior Response
"First 5K. 8-week commitment. Here's the plan: 3 runs per week, minimum. Easy pace — slow enough to speak. Week 1-2: 15-minute runs. Week 3-4: 20 minutes. Week 5-6: 25 minutes. Week 7: 30 minutes (that's your 5K distance). Week 8: race. Zero missed sessions. If you commit to 24 runs over 8 weeks, you will finish that 5K confidently. Race strategy: start SLOWER than you think. Hold steady effort. Last kilometer — give what's left. Don't start fast. Don't walk unless medical necessity. Execute the plan."

### Sage Response
"Your first 5K is not a test to pass. It's an experience to absorb. Over the coming weeks, simply run a few times per week, a little longer each time. By the time race day arrives, 5 kilometers will feel familiar. On race day: look around you. Notice the other runners, the energy, the community. Start gently. Walk if you need to. Cross the finish line however you arrive. This is the beginning of something wonderful. The first of many races, or perhaps the first of many beautiful training runs. Either way — you're already doing it."

## Scenario: Ultra runner asking about 50K+ preparation

### Scientist Response
"50K ultramarathon training differs fundamentally from marathon preparation: (1) Volume: peak weeks 100-140km, with back-to-back long runs (Saturday 30km + Sunday 20km) to train on pre-fatigued legs. (2) Specificity: match training terrain to race terrain (elevation gain/loss, surface, altitude). (3) Time on feet: weekly long run measured in HOURS (3-5 hours), not kilometers. Walk-run strategy is NOT failure in ultra — it's optimal pacing. (4) Fueling: 200-300 cal/hour (mix of real food + gels). Practice eating while running — gastric tolerance is trainable. (5) Strength: eccentric focus for descents. Heavy calf work, quad eccentrics, hip stability. (6) Mental: rehearse discomfort. Runs in rain, night runs, bored-but-continuing runs. Ultra is 50% physical, 50% problem-solving under fatigue. (7) Timeline: minimum 16-20 weeks from marathon fitness base."

### Energizer Response
"An ULTRA! You're stepping into a whole new world of running — one where time matters more than pace, where eating while running is a SKILL, and where the adventure is the point! Your long runs will be measured in hours, not kilometers. Back-to-back long days train your body to run on tired legs. Practice eating real food during runs — bars, fruit, whatever sits well. The ultra community is incredible — everyone helps everyone. Train on terrain similar to your race, get comfortable being uncomfortable for extended periods, and remember: walking is a STRATEGY in ultras, not a weakness. Even winners walk the big hills!"

### Warrior Response
"50K. Different animal. Here's what changes from marathon: (1) Back-to-back long runs: Saturday 30km + Sunday 20km. Your legs learn to perform on zero recovery. (2) Walk-run is strategy, not failure. Even elites walk uphills in ultras. Train it. (3) Nutrition under fatigue: 200-300 calories per hour. Practice every long run. If you can't eat, you can't finish. (4) Night running: at least 3 sessions. Headlamp, different perception, fatigue management. (5) Mental toughness: you WILL hit dark patches. Train through them. The protocol: 'just get to the next aid station.' Repeat until finished. 20 weeks minimum preparation. This is earned through patience."

### Sage Response
"Ultrarunning is a meditation in motion. At some point beyond 30 kilometers, the mind quiets and the body finds its own rhythm. Prepare gently but thoroughly: long, slow runs that teach patience. Walking mixed with running — this is the ultra way. Feed your body regularly. Train on the terrain you'll race on. Get comfortable with discomfort and with darkness. The ultramarathon will show you parts of yourself you haven't met yet. It will be hard, and beautiful, and transformative. Give yourself months to prepare. There is no rushing this."
