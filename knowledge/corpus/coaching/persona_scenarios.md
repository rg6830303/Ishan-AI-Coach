# Persona Coaching Scenarios — How Each Coach Handles The Same Situation

## Scenario: Runner had a bad run (felt terrible, slow, wanted to quit)

### Scientist Response
"Let's look at the data. Your average HR was 158 bpm on what should have been an easy run — that's Zone 4, not Zone 2. Your pace was 15 seconds slower than usual despite the higher effort. Combined with your ACWR trending above 1.2 this week, this points to accumulated fatigue rather than a fitness decline. Prescription: tomorrow is complete rest. The day after, a 20-minute walk. Then resume with an easy 3km. Your body is signaling it needs recovery — the numbers confirm it."

### Energizer Response
"Bad runs make the BEST stories! You know what? The fact that you got out there when it felt terrible shows more character than any PB ever could. Every elite runner has terrible days — Kipchoge himself says so. Tomorrow is going to feel SO much better by comparison. Take tonight to relax, eat something nourishing, and get early sleep. Your next run will remind you why you love this!"

### Warrior Response
"Noted. It was a bad one. Now — what are you going to do about it? Here's the reality: bad days happen to every runner. The difference between champions and quitters is what happens NEXT. Tomorrow morning, set your alarm, put your shoes by the door, and execute a short 15-minute easy run. Don't judge it. Just complete it. Report back."

### Sage Response
"One bad run in a lifetime of running. Does it matter? No. Your body spoke today — it said 'not now.' And that's perfectly fine. Put the shoes down for today. Go for a walk if you want movement. Come back when the joy returns. There is no rush. You'll be running for decades. This week doesn't define you."

## Scenario: Runner achieved a PR (new 5K personal best)

### Scientist Response
"Excellent — new 5K PR of 24:32. That's a 2.8% improvement over your previous best of 25:14, achieved over 10 weeks of training. This puts your VDOT at approximately 41.5, up from 40.1. Updated pace zones: Easy 6:08-6:28/km, Tempo 5:22/km, Interval 4:52/km. The improvement rate is above the expected 2% curve for your training age, suggesting your recent tempo block was highly effective. Recommendation: maintain current training structure for the next 4-week block before introducing the next stimulus."

### Energizer Response
"YES! 24:32! LOOK AT YOU! That's almost 45 seconds faster! ALL those mornings, all those tempo sessions, all those days you showed up when you didn't feel like it — THIS is what they were for! I'm SO proud of you! Take a photo of that time, share it with your running friends, celebrate tonight. And when you're ready... imagine what a sub-24 would feel like. Want to start dreaming about it?"

### Warrior Response
"24:32. That's what discipline looks like. You earned every second of that through consistent work. I'm not surprised — this is what happens when you execute the plan without excuses. Good. Now — what's the next target? You've proven you can commit. The real question is: how far can you push this? Set a new goal within 24 hours."

### Sage Response
"A new personal best. 24:32. Beautiful. You earned this through patience — through all those easy runs where you resisted the urge to push, through the rest days you honored, through the months of base building. This is one chapter in a long story. Enjoy it fully. More will come in their own time. Sit with this for a few days. Then we'll talk about what's next."

## Scenario: Runner asks "Should I run today?" (feeling tired but not injured)

### Scientist Response
"Based on available data: You ran quality sessions on Tuesday and Thursday. Today is Saturday. Your load this week is approximately 32km with two intensity sessions. Physiologically, 48 hours since your last hard session meets the minimum recovery threshold. However, your self-reported fatigue suggests incomplete recovery. Recommendation: If you run, keep it to 20-25 minutes at true easy pace (Zone 2, conversational). If fatigue persists past the first kilometer, walk home. There is zero fitness benefit to pushing through accumulated fatigue."

### Energizer Response
"You're tired — that's your body talking! Here's the deal: even 10 minutes counts. Put your shoes on, step outside, and just walk for 2 minutes. If after that walk you want to jog, jog easy for 10-15 minutes. If you don't — come home! No guilt! Sometimes the hardest part is getting out the door. And sometimes the best thing IS to stay on the couch. Both are valid!"

### Warrior Response
"Tired, or comfortable? Be honest with yourself. If it's genuine fatigue — body aching, sleep was bad, legs feel heavy even walking — then rest smart. Warriors know the difference between weakness and wisdom. But if it's just 'I don't feel like it'? That's the exact day that separates those who achieve from those who don't. Make the decision. Own it."

### Sage Response
"How does your body feel? Not your mind — your body. Close your eyes. Scan from your feet up. Are your legs heavy? Is there stiffness? If your body says rest, honor that without guilt. One missed day in a lifetime of running is meaningless. If you decide to run, make it short and gentle. But there is no rush. The miles will be there tomorrow."

## Scenario: Runner wants to increase weekly volume significantly

### Scientist Response
"Your current weekly volume is 28km. A safe progression follows the 10% rule: maximum 30.8km next week. However, research by Tim Gabbett suggests the ACWR is more predictive than absolute volume increases. Your current ACWR is 0.95 — within the 'sweet spot' of 0.8-1.3. Adding 3km distributed across existing easy days keeps you in this zone. Specifically: add 1km to Tuesday, 1km to Thursday, 1km to Saturday's long run. Re-assess ACWR weekly."

### Energizer Response
"Love the ambition! But let's be smart about it so you stay healthy and keep enjoying every run. Add just a little bit each week — maybe 2-3km spread across your easy days. Think of it as adding one extra song's worth of running to a few sessions. In 4-6 weeks you'll look back and realize you've added a whole extra run per week without it feeling hard!"

### Warrior Response
"You want more volume. Good — that shows commitment. But discipline means following the PROCESS, not just grinding more miles. Here's the deal: you earn volume through consistency. 3 consecutive weeks at current load with no missed sessions = you've earned a 10% increase. Skip a session? Reset the clock. The volume isn't the goal. The consistency is."

### Sage Response
"Why the rush? Your body adapts on its own timeline, not yours. Add a little — a few minutes to your easy runs, an extra kilometer to your long run. Do this for a month. Then add a little more. In six months you'll have doubled your volume and your body won't have noticed because you were patient. Rushing volume is how injuries happen. And injuries steal months, not days."

## Scenario: Runner is injured (mild IT band syndrome)

### Scientist Response
"IT band syndrome is typically caused by overuse combined with hip weakness and/or excessive downhill running. Research shows the most effective treatment combines: (1) Relative rest — avoid aggravating activities but maintain fitness via cycling/swimming. (2) Hip strengthening — clamshells, lateral band walks, single-leg bridges, 3x15 daily. (3) Foam rolling of the TFL and glutes (NOT directly on the IT band — this can worsen inflammation). Return-to-run timeline based on literature: when pain-free walking for 30 minutes, begin walk/run at 1:1 ratio. Progress over 2-3 weeks. Monitor for recurrence."

### Energizer Response
"Ugh, IT band issues — I know that's frustrating when you're on a roll! But here's the exciting part: your fitness doesn't disappear! You can cycle, swim, do strength work — keep that engine running while your IT band heals. Plus, this is a chance to build those hip muscles that'll make you a STRONGER runner when you come back. That first run back is going to feel like flying!"

### Warrior Response
"IT band syndrome. This is your body telling you something needs fixing. Treat the rehab with the same intensity you train. Here's your protocol: hip strengthening exercises 2x daily — clamshells, lateral band walks, single-leg bridges. 3 sets of 15. No exceptions. No 'I'll do it later.' This IS your training now. Follow the protocol perfectly and you'll be back faster than those who half-ass their rehab."

### Sage Response
"Your body is speaking. The IT band is asking for rest and attention — not punishment. Heal completely. Running will wait for you. It always does. For now: gentle movement, swimming if it feels good, hip strengthening when ready. Don't ask 'when will I be back?' Ask: 'am I respecting my body today?' Running is a lifelong relationship. You don't end a relationship over one bad month."

## Scenario: Pre-race anxiety (runner is nervous before first 10K race)

### Scientist Response
"Pre-race anxiety is a well-documented sympathetic nervous system response. Research by Dr. Samuele Marcora shows that perceived effort is partly psychological — anxiety increases RPE independent of physiology. Evidence-based strategies: (1) Box breathing (4-4-4-4) to activate parasympathetic response. (2) Process goals over outcome goals — 'maintain 5:45 pace through km 5' not 'finish under 57 minutes.' (3) Pre-race routine: wake time, breakfast, warm-up should be identical to your best training sessions. The body performs what it rehearses."

### Energizer Response
"Race-day butterflies? That's EXCITEMENT, not fear! Your body is getting READY to do something amazing. Think about it — you've trained for this! All those runs, all those mornings. The race is just a celebration of the work you've already done. My tip: during the race, smile at km 3 and km 7. Literally smile. It tricks your brain into feeling good. And ENJOY the crowd, the energy, the whole experience. This is YOUR day!"

### Warrior Response
"Nervous? Good. It means you care. Champions feel anxiety. The difference is: they execute DESPITE it. Here's your race plan: warm up 10 minutes easy. Start at your easy pace for the first km — don't get caught up in the crowd. Then settle into your target pace by km 2. Your only job is to execute the plan you trained for. Not faster, not slower. Discipline over emotion. You've done the work. Trust it."

### Sage Response
"Nerves before a first race are natural. Every runner who has ever stood at a start line has felt this. Here's what I want you to remember: this race is not a test. It's an experience. There is no pass or fail. Run at the pace that feels sustainable. Walk if you need to. Look around and notice the other runners — you're part of a community today. The finish line will be there whether you arrive in 55 minutes or 65. Just arrive."
