# Injury Prevention, Common Injuries & Return-to-Run

## Injury Prevention Principles

### The 80% Rule
80% of running injuries are overuse injuries caused by too much, too soon, or too often. The remaining 20% are acute (falls, ankle rolls). Prevention focuses on load management, not just stretching.

### Key Prevention Strategies
- Follow the 10% rule for weekly volume increases
- Include 2+ rest days per week for beginners, 1-2 for advanced
- Run on varied surfaces (not always concrete)
- Replace shoes every 500-800km
- Strength train 2x/week (hips, glutes, calves, core)
- Sleep 7-9 hours (tissue repair happens during sleep)
- Gradually introduce speed work (not before 3+ months of base)

### ACWR for Injury Prevention
Acute:Chronic Workload Ratio (7-day load / 28-day average) should stay between 0.8-1.3. Above 1.5 = high injury risk. Sudden spikes (vacation → high volume return) are the #1 cause of overuse injury.

## Common Running Injuries

### Runner's Knee (Patellofemoral Pain Syndrome)
**Symptoms:** Pain around/behind kneecap, worse going downstairs, after prolonged sitting, or during running.
**Cause:** Weak quads/glutes, overpronation, sudden volume increase, tight IT band.
**Treatment:** Reduce volume 30-50%. Strengthen VMO (quad), glute medius. Shorten stride length. Avoid deep squats initially. Ice after runs.
**Return:** Pain-free daily activity → walk → walk-run → easy running. 2-6 weeks typical.

### Shin Splints (Medial Tibial Stress Syndrome)
**Symptoms:** Pain along inner shin bone, worse at start of run, may ease during then return after.
**Cause:** Too much too soon, hard surfaces, worn shoes, tight calves, weak tibialis anterior.
**Treatment:** Rest from running (cross-train with cycling/swimming). Calf raises, toe raises. Ice. Compression. New shoes if >500km.
**Return:** When pain-free walking → 50% volume, flat surfaces only. If persistent >3 weeks: rule out stress fracture with imaging.
**RED FLAG:** Point tenderness (one specific spot) vs diffuse pain. Point tenderness = possible stress fracture → medical imaging needed.

### Plantar Fasciitis
**Symptoms:** Heel/arch pain, worst with first steps in morning, improves with movement then worsens with prolonged activity.
**Cause:** Tight calves, weak foot intrinsics, sudden increase in running/standing, high BMI, minimalist shoes too soon.
**Treatment:** Rolling foot on frozen bottle (2 min, 3x/day). Calf stretches (bent and straight knee). Night splint for severe cases. Supportive shoes. Avoid walking barefoot on hard floors.
**Return:** Pain <3/10 during running → maintain. If >5/10 → stop and reassess. Recovery: 3-12 months for full resolution. Can run through mild cases with management.

### IT Band Syndrome
**Symptoms:** Sharp pain on outer knee, typically appears at same distance every run, worse downhill.
**Cause:** Weak glute medius, excessive hip drop (Trendelenburg), too much downhill, sudden volume increase.
**Treatment:** Reduce volume. Glute med strengthening (clamshells, side-lying abduction, single-leg bridges). Foam rolling ABOVE the IT band (TFL, glute), not the band itself. Avoid cambered roads.
**Return:** Pain-free 20 minutes → gradually extend. 3-8 weeks typical.

### Achilles Tendinopathy
**Symptoms:** Stiffness/pain in Achilles tendon, worse in morning, may improve during run then worsen after. May have a thickened area (nodule).
**Cause:** Sudden increase in hills/speed, tight calves, weak calves, age (>35), previous injury.
**Treatment:** Eccentric heel drops (Alfredson protocol: 3x15 reps, 2x daily, for 12 weeks). Reduce hills and speed. Do NOT rest completely — controlled loading promotes tendon remodeling. Avoid stretching an irritable tendon.
**Return:** Manage with pain monitoring: <3/10 during = okay to run. Not worse 24h after = okay. Increase by 10% weekly. Full recovery: 3-6 months.
**IMPORTANT:** Complete rest makes tendinopathy worse. Tendons need progressive loading to heal.

### Stress Fracture
**Symptoms:** Point tenderness on bone. Pain worsens with activity, present at rest in severe cases. Night pain. Pain with hopping on one leg.
**Cause:** Rapid volume increase, low energy availability (RED-S), female athlete triad, low bone density, vitamin D deficiency, thin shoes.
**Treatment:** Complete rest from impact for 4-8 weeks (depending on location and grade). Cross-train: swimming, cycling only. Medical imaging (MRI) for confirmation. Address underlying cause (nutrition, hormones, training load).
**Return:** Pain-free walking 2 weeks → elliptical/aqua jogging → return-to-run protocol over 4-6 weeks starting at 50% volume, flat surfaces, soft ground.
**RED FLAGS:** Femoral neck stress fracture = surgical emergency risk. Any hip/groin point tenderness with running → STOP and get imaging immediately.

### Hamstring Strain
**Symptoms:** Sudden sharp pain in back of thigh during speed work or hills. Bruising may appear.
**Cause:** Speed work without warmup, fatigue, previous strain (re-injury rate high), weak hamstrings relative to quads.
**Treatment:** RICE for 48h. Gentle stretching after 72h. Progressive loading: isometrics → eccentrics → running. Nordic hamstring curls for prevention.
**Return:** Grade 1 (mild): 1-3 weeks. Grade 2 (moderate): 4-8 weeks. Grade 3 (severe/complete): 3-6 months.

## Red Flag Symptoms — STOP Running Immediately

These require medical attention, not coaching:
- **Chest pain or pressure during exercise** → cardiac assessment
- **Sudden severe headache during exertion** → possible vascular event
- **Difficulty breathing disproportionate to effort** → cardiac/pulmonary
- **Sudden joint locking or giving way** → possible meniscus/ligament
- **Point tenderness on bone with swelling** → possible fracture
- **Numbness/tingling in extremities during running** → nerve compression
- **Blood in urine after running** → kidney/bladder assessment
- **Sharp localized pain that worsens with every step** → stop, don't push through
- **Persistent chest/arm pain post-exercise** → emergency

## Return-to-Run Protocols

### After Illness (Cold/Flu/COVID)
**Wait:** Fever-free for 48+ hours before ANY exercise.
**Days 1-2:** Walk only (20-30 min).
**Days 3-4:** Easy jog, 50% of normal volume, flat terrain.
**Days 5-7:** Gradual rebuild to 75% volume. No intensity.
**Week 2:** Return to normal easy volume. No intensity for 14 days post-fever.
**POST-COVID SPECIFIC:** If chest symptoms during illness, get cardiac clearance before returning. Myocarditis risk is real. No exercise for minimum 10 days post-positive test if symptomatic.

### After Extended Break (2+ weeks off)
**Rule:** For every week off, take 2-3 days to rebuild.
- 2 weeks off → start at 50%, rebuild over 5-6 days
- 4 weeks off → start at 30%, rebuild over 2 weeks
- 8+ weeks off → treat as beginner restart (walk/run)

### After Injury (General)
1. Pain-free daily activities for 5+ consecutive days
2. Walk 30 minutes pain-free
3. Walk-run (1 min run / 2 min walk) for 20 min, assess 24h later
4. If no flare: progress run intervals (2:1, then 3:1, then continuous)
5. Rebuild to 50% of pre-injury volume over 2 weeks
6. Add 10% weekly back to full volume
7. Intensity last — minimum 2 weeks of easy running before any quality sessions

## Strength Training for Runners

### Essential Exercises (2x/week, 20-30 min)
- **Single-leg calf raises** (3x15 each) — Achilles and calf strength
- **Single-leg bridges** (3x12 each) — glute activation
- **Clamshells with band** (3x15 each) — glute medius
- **Step-ups** (3x10 each) — quad and hip strength
- **Plank** (3x30-60s) — core stability
- **Copenhagen adductors** (3x8 each) — groin injury prevention
- **Nordic hamstring curls** (3x5) — hamstring injury prevention

### When to Do It
After easy runs or on rest days. Never before quality sessions (fatigue reduces running economy). Prioritize hip/glute work — most running injuries trace back to weak hips.
