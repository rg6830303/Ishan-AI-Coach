# Energy Systems, Heart Rate Zones & Training Intensity

## The Three Energy Systems in Detail

### System 1: Phosphocreatine (ATP-PC) — 0-10 seconds
- Stored ATP + creatine phosphate in muscle cells
- Instant energy, no oxygen needed, no byproducts
- Depletes in 6-10 seconds of all-out effort
- Full recovery: 3-5 minutes
- Running relevance: race start, sprint finish (final 50-100m kick)
- Training: short hill sprints (8-10s), strides, plyometrics

### System 2: Anaerobic Glycolysis — 10 seconds to 2 minutes
- Glucose → pyruvate → lactate (without oxygen)
- High power output but produces hydrogen ions (the "burn")
- Dominant in 400m-800m racing
- Byproduct: lactate + H+ ions → acidosis → muscle failure
- Recovery: 2-5 minutes for partial, 20+ minutes for full clearance
- Running relevance: hard interval reps, race surges, final km push
- Training: 200-600m reps at faster than 5K pace with full recovery

### System 3: Aerobic Oxidation — 2 minutes to unlimited
- Oxygen + fuel (glycogen or fat) → ATP + CO2 + H2O
- Lower power but virtually unlimited duration
- Dominant for ALL running events beyond 800m
- Fuel sources shift with intensity:
  - Low intensity (<60% VO2max): primarily fat
  - Moderate (60-80%): mix of fat and glycogen
  - High (>85%): primarily glycogen
  - Maximum: almost entirely glycogen
- Running relevance: 95%+ of all training and racing energy
- Training: everything from easy runs to tempo to long runs

## The Crossover Concept (Brooks, 1994)

### What It Means
At rest: ~60% fat, 40% carbs. As intensity rises, the balance shifts toward carbs. The "crossover point" is where carbs become the dominant fuel source — typically around 60-65% VO2max for untrained, 70-75% for trained runners.

### Why It Matters for Running
- Training below the crossover → builds fat oxidation capacity → spares glycogen
- Training above → builds glycolytic capacity but depletes glycogen faster
- Well-trained runners have a HIGHER crossover point → can run faster on fat
- Marathon runners MUST develop fat oxidation — glycogen alone won't last 42km
- This is why easy running isn't "junk miles" — it's building your fat-burning engine

### Practical Application
- MAF/Zone 2 training shifts the crossover point rightward (more fat at higher intensities)
- Takes 3-6 months of consistent easy running to see significant improvement
- Measured by: pace at fat-max (RER/respiratory exchange ratio test), or pace at MAF HR

## Heart Rate Zones — Detailed

### Zone 1: Active Recovery (50-60% max HR)
- Purpose: Promote blood flow without training stress. Recovery between hard days.
- Feel: Can sing. Barely feels like exercise. Many runners can't run this slow (walk/run is fine).
- Physiological effect: Parasympathetic activation, gentle capillary flow, minimal glycogen use.
- Duration: 20-40 minutes. Not meant for long sessions.
- Who uses it: Recovery day. Day after race. Day after hard intervals.

### Zone 2: Aerobic Base (60-70% max HR)
- Purpose: Build mitochondria, capillary density, fat oxidation, cardiac stroke volume.
- Feel: Fully conversational. Could maintain for hours. Feels "too easy" for many runners.
- Physiological effect: Maximum mitochondrial biogenesis, stroke volume development, fat oxidation training.
- Duration: 30 minutes to 2+ hours. The more time here, the better your engine.
- Who uses it: EVERYONE. 80% of training should live here. The most important zone.
- Common mistake: Running Zone 3 and calling it Zone 2. If you can't hold a full conversation, you're too fast.

### Zone 3: Tempo / Moderate (70-80% max HR)
- Purpose: Develop lactate clearance. Improve "threshold" pace.
- Feel: "Comfortably hard." Can speak in short phrases (3-5 words) but not full sentences.
- Physiological effect: LT2 improvement, improved lactate shuttling between muscle fibers.
- Duration: 20-40 minutes continuous (classic tempo run) or cruise intervals (3x10 min).
- Who uses it: Structured quality sessions 1-2x/week. NOT easy days.
- Warning: The "grey zone" in 80/20 training. Too hard for base building, too easy for VO2max stimulus. Don't accidentally live here.

### Zone 4: Threshold / VO2max Development (80-90% max HR)
- Purpose: Develop VO2max, improve maximal oxygen uptake, speed at LT2.
- Feel: Hard. Can only say a few words. Counting down reps. Breathing is labored.
- Physiological effect: Central cardiovascular adaptation, increased cardiac output, VO2max gains.
- Duration: 3-5 minute intervals with equal recovery. Total quality time: 15-25 min per session.
- Who uses it: 1-2x/week during build/sharpen phase. Classic: 5x1000m, 4x1200m, 3x1600m.
- Warning: Very fatiguing. Requires 48-72h recovery. Overuse → overtraining.

### Zone 5: Neuromuscular / Speed (90-100% max HR)
- Purpose: Improve running economy, neuromuscular recruitment, anaerobic power.
- Feel: Maximum effort. Can't speak. Sprinting or near-sprinting.
- Physiological effect: Fast-twitch fiber recruitment, stride power, running form under fatigue.
- Duration: 30-90 second reps with full recovery (2-3 min). Total: 5-12 min per session.
- Who uses it: 1x/week maximum. Strides (6-8x20s) count. Hill sprints count. 200m reps count.
- Warning: Highest injury risk. Only when fully warmed up. Never on tired legs.

## Calculating Max Heart Rate

### Formulas (all are estimates, ±10-12 bpm error)
- **Traditional:** 220 - age (too simple, widely inaccurate)
- **Tanaka (2001):** 208 - (0.7 x age) — more accurate for adults
- **Gulati (women):** 206 - (0.88 x age) — specific to women
- **Best method:** Field test. Warm up 15 min, then run a steep hill 3x2 min at maximum effort with 2 min recovery. Highest HR in final rep ≈ max HR.

### Why Formulas Fail
- Individual variation is ±10-12 bpm from any formula
- A 35-year-old might have max HR of 175 or 195 — formula says 185
- Training history, genetics, and heart size all affect max HR
- Using wrong max HR → wrong zones → wrong training intensity → plateaus or overtraining

### Heart Rate Reserve (Karvonen Method)
Target HR = Resting HR + (% intensity × (Max HR - Resting HR))
- More accurate than raw %maxHR because it accounts for fitness (lower resting HR = fitter)
- Example: Resting 55, Max 190, targeting 70% intensity:
  55 + (0.70 × (190 - 55)) = 55 + 94.5 = 149 bpm
- This is closer to actual metabolic intensity than straight %maxHR

## RPE (Rate of Perceived Exertion) — The Underrated Tool

### Why RPE Matters
- Heart rate lags effort by 30-60 seconds (useless for intervals)
- Heart rate affected by: caffeine, heat, dehydration, stress, altitude, sleep
- RPE is IMMEDIATE and accounts for all variables automatically
- Elite runners use RPE more than HR (they know their body)

### Borg 6-20 Scale (Original)
| RPE | Description | Zone Equivalent |
|-----|-------------|-----------------|
| 6-8 | Very very light | Rest/walking |
| 9-11 | Very light to light | Zone 1 |
| 12-13 | Somewhat hard | Zone 2 (easy run) |
| 14-15 | Hard | Zone 3 (tempo) |
| 16-17 | Very hard | Zone 4 (intervals) |
| 18-19 | Very very hard | Zone 5 (max) |
| 20 | Maximum exertion | All-out sprint |

### Modified 1-10 Scale (More Common in Running)
| RPE | Description | Training Application |
|-----|-------------|---------------------|
| 1-2 | Barely moving | Walking/recovery |
| 3-4 | Easy, could do all day | Easy runs, Zone 2 |
| 5-6 | Moderate, aware of effort | Tempo/threshold |
| 7-8 | Hard, wanting to slow | VO2max intervals |
| 9 | Very hard, almost failing | Final interval rep |
| 10 | Maximum, can't go harder | Sprint finish |

### Using RPE in Training
- Easy runs: RPE 3-4 (if it feels harder, slow down regardless of pace)
- Tempo: RPE 5-6 (sustained discomfort, not agony)
- Intervals: RPE 7-8 (hard but controlled, can complete all reps)
- Reps/speed: RPE 9 (near maximum, form still decent)
- Race 5K: RPE 8-9 sustained. Race marathon: RPE 6-7 sustained.

## Training Intensity Distribution

### The 80/20 Principle (by time)
- 80% Zone 1-2 (easy): Build the engine without accumulating fatigue
- 0% Zone 3 (moderate): The "dead zone" — avoid spending time here on purpose
- 20% Zone 4-5 (hard): Stimulate adaptation with focused quality

### Why Most Runners Get This Wrong
- Average recreational runner: 50% Zone 2, 40% Zone 3, 10% Zone 4-5
- They run easy days too hard and hard days too easy → "moderate all the time"
- Result: chronic fatigue, plateau, injury, burnout
- Fix: Make easy days EMBARRASSINGLY slow. Make hard days genuinely HARD. Nothing in between.

### Polarization by Training Phase
- **Base phase:** 90/10 (or even 95/5). Almost all easy. Build volume.
- **Build phase:** 80/20. Introduction of structured quality.
- **Peak/sharpen:** 75/25. More intensity, less volume.
- **Taper:** 80/20 but reduced volume across both.
- **Recovery phase:** 100% easy or complete rest.

## Power in Running (Newer Metric)

### What Running Power Measures
Wattage output via accelerometry (Stryd, Apple Watch, Garmin). Unlike pace (affected by hills, wind, terrain), power reflects ACTUAL mechanical output.

### Why It's Useful
- Pacing on hills: same power uphill and downhill (pace changes but effort stays constant)
- Wind: same power into headwind (pace drops but you're working the same)
- Trail running: pace is meaningless, power is consistent
- Training zones: similar to cycling power zones (threshold power = FTP equivalent)

### Limitations
- Not as well-validated as cycling power (running mechanics are more complex)
- Different devices give different numbers (not standardized like cycling)
- Most runners don't need it — HR + RPE + pace covers 95% of needs
- Best for: trail runners, hill-heavy training, and runners who want precision pacing

## Lactate Testing for Runners

### What a Lactate Test Reveals
- Your exact LT1 (aerobic threshold) and LT2 (anaerobic threshold) in pace AND heart rate
- Your training zones based on YOUR physiology (not formulas)
- Whether your easy pace is truly easy (many runners discover their "easy" is above LT1)
- Where to focus training (low LT2? → more tempo work. Low VO2max? → more intervals)

### When to Get Tested
- Before a major training block (to set accurate zones)
- Every 3-4 months (thresholds shift with training)
- If you've plateaued (may reveal your "easy" is too hard or "hard" is too easy)
- Not necessary for beginners (RPE + talk test is sufficient initially)

### DIY Threshold Estimation
If no lab access:
- **LT2 pace:** The pace you could hold for exactly 60 minutes in a race (roughly 15K-half marathon race pace for trained runners)
- **LT1 pace:** The fastest pace at which you could still speak in full sentences
- **VO2max pace:** Your current 3K-5K race pace
