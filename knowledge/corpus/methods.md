# Training Methods & Philosophies

## Jack Daniels / VDOT System

### Core Philosophy
Every training pace should be tied to a measurable output (VDOT score derived from race performance). No guessing. The training zones are: Easy (E), Marathon (M), Threshold (T), Interval (I), Repetition (R). Each targets a specific physiological adaptation.

### Key Principles
- VDOT is calculated from your best recent race time
- Each training zone has a specific purpose — never train in between zones
- E pace: builds aerobic base (60-74% VO2max). Most mileage lives here.
- T pace: improves lactate clearance (83-88% VO2max). Cruise intervals: 3-4x10 min.
- I pace: develops VO2max (95-100%). Classic: 5x1000m at 5K pace.
- R pace: improves speed/economy (>105%). Short reps: 200-400m with full recovery.
- Weekly quality should not exceed 10% of total mileage at I+R pace
- Jack's Law: never increase weekly volume more than 1 hour per year of running experience

### When to Apply
Best for: runners with a recent race time who want structured, science-based training. Strong for 5K-marathon preparation. Less useful for complete beginners (who don't have a VDOT yet).

## Pfitzinger — Advanced Marathoning

### Core Philosophy
Marathon-specific preparation through progressive long runs, medium-long runs, and lactate threshold work. The program builds volume methodically while incorporating marathon-pace running early and often.

### Key Principles
- Medium-long runs (13-17km) twice per week as the training backbone
- Long runs progress: start at 2h, build to 3h+. Last 25% at marathon pace progression.
- Lactate threshold: 25-40 min at 15K-half pace. The "meat" of quality work.
- VO2max intervals only during the sharpening phase (final 6 weeks)
- Recovery runs capped at 60-75% of marathon pace
- 5 days/week minimum for half marathon, 6 for marathon
- 18-week programs (12-week base + 18-week specific)

### When to Apply
Best for: intermediate-to-advanced runners training for half marathon or marathon. Requires 5-6 days/week commitment. Not suitable for runners with <30km/week base.

## Arthur Lydiard — Base Building

### Core Philosophy
Build an enormous aerobic base before adding ANY speed work. The aerobic system is the foundation of all running performance. Mileage builds the engine; speed work tunes it.

### Key Principles
- 10-12 weeks of pure aerobic running (no intervals, no tempo) to start
- Run as much as you can recover from at aerobic pace
- "Train, don't strain" — aerobic running should feel moderate, not exhausting
- Long run every week (up to 2+ hours) at comfortable pace
- Hill resistance phase after base: steep hill repetitions for strength
- Speed/anaerobic phase comes LAST (4-6 weeks before target race)
- The base NEVER goes away — always return to it between racing phases

### When to Apply
Best for: building a huge aerobic foundation. Excellent for comeback periods, off-season, or runners who've never built a proper base. Also excellent for masters runners who benefit from volume over intensity.

## Norwegian Double Threshold (Ingebrigtsen Method)

### Core Philosophy
Train the lactate threshold with extremely high frequency (5-6 threshold sessions per week for elites) at precisely measured blood lactate levels. The sessions are submaximal in isolation — it's the frequency that creates adaptation, not the single-session intensity.

### Key Principles
- Threshold defined by blood lactate (typically 2-4 mmol/L) not pace or feel
- Intervals at threshold: 4x8 min, 5x6 min, 6x5 min — with 1-2 min recovery
- Each session is NOT maximal — runner should feel "I could do one more rep"
- High frequency: 10-12 quality sessions per week (for elites doing doubles)
- For non-elites: adapt to 3-4 threshold sessions per week
- Easy days are TRULY easy (much slower than threshold)
- Total volume still high (120-180km/week for elites)

### When to Apply
Best for: advanced runners who have plateaued on traditional 2x/week quality approaches. Requires lactate meter or well-calibrated RPE. NOT suitable for beginners or injury-prone runners (too much intensity frequency without experience). Research-backed but sample size is small (Norwegian national team).

## 80/20 Polarized Training (Stephen Seiler)

### Core Philosophy
80% of training time at LOW intensity (Zone 1-2), 20% at HIGH intensity (Zone 4-5). Almost ZERO time in the moderate "grey zone" (Zone 3). This produces better adaptations than threshold-heavy approaches.

### Key Principles
- Measure by TIME not distance (80% of sessions or time in Zone 1-2)
- Zone 3 (moderate) is the enemy — too hard to recover from, too easy to adapt to
- High-intensity portion: intervals, repetitions, hard fartlek (not "moderate tempo")
- Easy really means EASY — most runners' "easy" is actually Zone 3
- The 80/20 split is about the training PLAN, not every single run
- Supports by research across cycling, rowing, swimming, running — universal principle

### When to Apply
Best for: any runner at any level. The most broadly applicable training philosophy. Particularly important for runners who "always train hard" and plateau (they need more easy, not more hard). Backed by the strongest body of research in endurance training.

## MAF Method (Phil Maffetone)

### Core Philosophy
Train exclusively below the Maximum Aerobic Function (MAF) heart rate (180 - age ± adjustments). This develops fat oxidation and aerobic efficiency without stress. Over months, pace at the same HR improves significantly.

### Key Principles
- MAF HR = 180 - age (subtract 5 if injured/ill, add 5 if 2+ years consistent training)
- ALL running below MAF HR. No exceptions for months.
- Track progress: monthly MAF test (same route, note pace at MAF HR — should get faster)
- No structured intervals, tempo, or speed work during MAF period
- May be painfully slow initially (walk/run for some) — trust the process
- Duration: 3-6 months minimum for full aerobic development
- After base: can add moderate intensity while maintaining MAF principles

### When to Apply
Best for: runners returning from injury, burnt-out runners, beginners building base, runners with chronic overtraining. Excellent reset. NOT ideal for: race-specific sharpening phases or runners who need speed development. Long-term exclusive MAF without any intensity leads to neuromuscular detraining.

## Hansons Method

### Core Philosophy
Cumulative fatigue replicates race conditions better than single long runs. The program uses shorter long runs (16 miles max for marathon, not 20-22) but runs them on tired legs, simulating the late-race state.

### Key Principles
- Long run capped at 16 miles / 26km for marathon (heresy vs Pfitzinger's 35km)
- Rationale: running 16 on fatigued legs from the week's training = running 20+ on fresh legs
- "Something of everything" — includes easy, tempo, speed, strength, long runs every week
- 6 days/week running (non-negotiable for the program to work)
- Cumulative weekly mileage matters more than any single run
- Speed work early in the plan (develops mechanics), marathon-pace work later
- Tempo runs at goal marathon pace — builds confidence and metabolic conditioning

### When to Apply
Best for: time-limited runners who can't do 3-hour long runs, runners prone to injury from very long runs, first-time marathoners who are intimidated by 32km training runs. Requires high weekly frequency (6 days).

## Canova Method

### Core Philosophy
Race-specific preparation through "special blocks" — extended periods of running at or near race pace. The marathon is prepared by running marathon pace for progressively longer portions of training runs. Specificity is king.

### Key Principles
- "Specific endurance" over general endurance — train the exact demands of your race
- Long runs with large MP portions: start easy, finish at/below MP for final 30-60 min
- Special blocks: 2-3 weeks of concentrated race-pace work
- Block periodization within mesocycles (not linear buildup)
- High overall volume (elite: 180-220km/week) with quality integrated into long runs
- Recovery is built-in: every hard block followed by absorption phase

### When to Apply
Best for: advanced marathon runners (sub-3:15) with high volume tolerance. Requires ability to run 30+ km with tempo portions. NOT for beginners or low-volume runners — the method requires extensive base and years of running.

## Choosing a Method — Decision Framework

| Runner Level | Goal | Recommended Primary Method |
|---|---|---|
| Beginner | First 5K | Lydiard base (pure aerobic) |
| Beginner | First 10K | 80/20 + simple progression |
| Intermediate | 5K/10K PR | Daniels VDOT system |
| Intermediate | First Half | Pfitzinger (shorter programs) |
| Advanced | Half PR | Norwegian threshold + Daniels |
| Advanced | Marathon | Pfitzinger or Hansons |
| Elite | Marathon PR | Canova specific blocks |
| Comeback/Injured | Rebuild | MAF method (3-6 months) |
| Plateau/Overtrained | Reset | Lydiard base → 80/20 |
| Masters (50+) | Health + performance | 80/20 + Lydiard principles |

### Blending Methods
No method is complete alone. Most successful coaches blend:
- Lydiard base → Daniels sharpening → Pfitzinger race-specific
- 80/20 overall split with Daniels-defined paces
- Norwegian frequency concepts at lower total volume for non-elites
- MAF rebuild phase → gradual transition to structured training
