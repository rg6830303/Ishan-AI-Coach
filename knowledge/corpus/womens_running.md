# Women's Running — Physiology, Cycle Training & Pregnancy

## Female-Specific Running Physiology

### Key Differences from Male Physiology
- Higher body fat percentage at equivalent fitness (essential fat: 12% women vs 3% men)
- Lower VO2max on average (due to lower hemoglobin, smaller heart/lung volume, higher essential fat)
- Greater reliance on fat oxidation at submaximal intensities (advantage for ultra-distance)
- More flexible connective tissue (ACL/ligament injury risk higher, joint hypermobility more common)
- Smaller glycogen stores → earlier glycogen depletion at same relative intensity
- Thermoregulation: higher core temp threshold before sweating begins in luteal phase

### Where Women Excel
- Ultra-endurance (50K+): performance gap narrows significantly vs men (fat oxidation advantage)
- Pacing discipline: women negative-split more often than men in marathons
- Recovery: some evidence of faster recovery between sessions (anti-inflammatory estrogen effect)
- Longevity: performance decline with age is slightly less steep in women

## Menstrual Cycle and Training

### The Phases (28-day average cycle)

#### Follicular Phase (Day 1-14, Menstruation through Ovulation)
- **Day 1-5 (Menstruation):** Low estrogen + low progesterone. Energy may be low. Iron loss.
- **Day 6-14 (Mid-follicular to Ovulation):** Rising estrogen. Generally BEST phase for hard training.
  - Higher pain tolerance
  - Better coordination/reaction time
  - Greater capacity for strength gains
  - Glycogen storage enhanced
  - Mood often elevated

#### Ovulation (Day ~14)
- Peak estrogen spike. Brief window of highest performance capacity.
- BUT: highest ACL injury risk (estrogen increases ligament laxity). Be careful with sudden direction changes.

#### Luteal Phase (Day 15-28)
- **Early luteal (Day 15-21):** Rising progesterone + still-high estrogen.
  - Core temp rises 0.3-0.5°C → higher resting HR (5-10 bpm), earlier perceived fatigue
  - Higher ventilation rate at same effort
  - Greater fat oxidation but reduced carb access → may need more carbs before hard sessions
  - Some women feel fine; others notice clear performance dip
  
- **Late luteal / PMS (Day 22-28):** Dropping hormones, possible symptoms.
  - Bloating, cramping, mood changes, fatigue, cravings
  - Higher RPE at same pace
  - Sleep disruption possible
  - Not ideal for PBs or key quality sessions (for MOST women — individual variation is huge)

### Cycle-Aware Training Strategy

#### Option A: Periodize Around the Cycle
- **Follicular (Day 6-14):** Schedule hardest sessions (intervals, tempo, heavy strength, tests)
- **Ovulation (Day ~14):** High-performance day, but warm up thoroughly (ligament risk)
- **Early luteal (Day 15-21):** Moderate training. Threshold work is fine. Expect +5-10 bpm HR.
- **Late luteal / PMS (Day 22-28):** Reduce intensity expectations. Easy/moderate focus. More recovery.
- **Menstruation (Day 1-5):** Individual. Many women train normally. Others need lighter days. Both are valid.

#### Option B: Listen to the Body
- Track symptoms, not just dates (cycle length varies, not every cycle is 28 days)
- If you feel great on Day 3 of your period: train hard. Hormones don't mandate behavior.
- If you feel terrible on Day 10: rest. Stress, sleep, and nutrition override cycle phase.
- Use a tracking app (Fitr Woman, Wild AI, or simple notes) to identify YOUR patterns over 3+ months

### Practical Tips
- **Iron:** Check ferritin annually. Heavy periods + running = double iron loss. Supplement if <30 ng/mL.
- **Pre-luteal hard sessions:** Extra 20-30g carbs before intensity (offset reduced glycogen access)
- **Bloating:** Magnesium (300-400mg/day in luteal phase), reduce salt, increase water
- **Cramps during runs:** Ibuprofen 30 min before (short-term, discuss with doctor). Gentle movement often helps more than rest.
- **Oral contraceptives:** Suppress natural cycle. Hormone levels are constant. Eliminates phase-based variation but may slightly reduce VO2max and alter thermoregulation.

## Pregnancy and Running

### First Trimester (Weeks 1-12)
- **Can you run?** YES if you were running before pregnancy AND feel well. Do NOT start running during pregnancy if not already a runner.
- **Adjustments:** Slow pace by 10-20%. Run by effort, not pace. Hydrate more.
- **Concerns:** Nausea may limit training. Fatigue is normal. Miscarriage risk is NOT increased by moderate exercise (this is a myth).
- **When to stop:** Bleeding, dizziness, severe nausea, doctor advises against.

### Second Trimester (Weeks 13-27)
- **Can you run?** Usually the "golden trimester" — nausea passes, energy returns, bump isn't too large yet.
- **Adjustments:** Ligaments are looser (relaxin hormone) → avoid unstable surfaces, sudden direction changes. Reduce intensity to conversational. Shorten stride as bump grows.
- **Pelvic floor:** Start pelvic floor exercises NOW. Running increases pelvic floor load.
- **When to stop:** Pelvic pain, urinary leakage during running, round ligament pain that doesn't resolve.

### Third Trimester (Weeks 28-40)
- **Can you run?** Some women run until week 38+. Others switch to walking/swimming by week 28. Both are fine.
- **Adjustments:** Walk/run is smart. Bladder pressure = frequent stops. Support belt may help. Reduce to 3-4x/week max.
- **Switch to walking/swimming when:** Running feels uncomfortable, pelvic floor is stressed, balance is affected, doctor recommends.
- **Priority:** Baby's health > fitness. Any discomfort = stop.

### Postpartum Return to Running

#### The Guidelines (UK guidelines for return to running post-birth)
- **0-6 weeks:** Rest and recovery. Walking only. Pelvic floor exercises daily.
- **6 weeks:** GP clearance for exercise. Does NOT mean "cleared to run."
- **6-12 weeks:** Low-impact exercise. Walking, swimming, cycling. Strength training (bodyweight). Pelvic floor rehab.
- **12 weeks minimum:** Earliest return to running IF: no pelvic floor symptoms, no pain, cleared by physio.
- **Many women:** 4-6 months before running feels comfortable. This is NORMAL.

#### Red Flags (Do NOT run if present)
- Urinary leakage during coughing/sneezing/jumping
- Pelvic heaviness or pressure
- Abdominal separation (diastasis recti) > 2 finger widths
- Pain in pelvis, hips, or lower back
- Bleeding that restarts with exercise

#### Rebuilding After Birth
- Start with walk/run (1 min run : 2 min walk for 20 min)
- Progress: 2:1, 3:1, continuous easy run
- Return to pre-pregnancy volume: expect 3-6 months minimum
- Return to intensity: only after volume is rebuilt
- Sleep deprivation = impaired recovery. Adjust expectations. Grace > goals.
- Breastfeeding: extra 300-500 cal/day + hydration. Running doesn't reduce milk supply if adequately fueled.

## Bra and Gear Considerations

### Sports Bra (Non-Trivial Performance Factor)
- Breast tissue has no internal support structure — ALL support comes from external compression
- Unsupported movement: up to 15cm per stride at D+ cup → pain, tissue damage, performance distraction
- Encapsulation > compression for C+ cup sizes
- Replace every 30-40 washes (elasticity degrades)
- Fit: should feel firm but not restrictive of breathing. Two-finger rule under band.
- For larger busts: look for separate cups, wide straps, back closure, high-impact rated

### Other Gear Notes
- Shorts/tights with inner brief: prevents chafing
- Anti-chafe: under bra band and inner thighs (Vaseline or BodyGlide)
- Period products for running: menstrual cup or disc (no movement), period underwear, tampon (pad shifts)
- Night running: reflective gear + headlamp + safe route. Tell someone your route.
- Personal safety: share location live (Strava Beacon, phone share). Run with phone. Vary routes.

## Common Myths — Debunked

### "Running during your period is bad for you"
FALSE. Exercise often reduces cramp severity and improves mood during menstruation. Listen to your body — if you feel good, run. If you don't, rest without guilt.

### "Running makes you lose your period (and that's fine)"
DANGEROUS MYTH. Amenorrhea (lost period) from running = RED-S = bone loss + hormonal disruption. This is a medical issue, not a sign of fitness. See a doctor immediately.

### "Strength training will make female runners bulky"
FALSE. Women have 10-20x less testosterone than men. Heavy lifting improves running economy without adding bulk at running volumes. The "bulky" fear causes women to undertrain strength — resulting in higher injury rates.

### "You shouldn't run while pregnant"
MOSTLY FALSE. Running during a healthy pregnancy is safe and beneficial (with modifications). Guidelines recommend continuing activities you did pre-pregnancy. Only stop if medical complications arise or doctor advises.

### "Women can't run ultras as well as men"
INCREASINGLY FALSE. The gender performance gap narrows dramatically beyond marathon distance. In 100-mile races, top women regularly beat top men. At 200+ miles, women may have a physiological advantage (fat oxidation + pacing discipline + pain tolerance).
