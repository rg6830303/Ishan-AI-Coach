# Recovery Science for Runners

## Sleep — The #1 Recovery Tool

### Why Sleep Matters More Than Any Supplement
Growth hormone secretion peaks during deep sleep (stages 3-4). This is when muscle repair, glycogen restoration, and neural adaptation occur. Inadequate sleep (<7h) increases injury risk by 60% (Milewski et al., 2014), impairs glycogen restoration by 30%, and reduces VO2max by 2-3%.

### Sleep Guidelines for Runners
- **Minimum:** 7 hours. **Optimal:** 8-9 hours during heavy training.
- **Consistency:** Same bedtime/wake time daily (circadian rhythm optimizes hormone release).
- **Naps:** 20-30 min nap post-hard training boosts recovery. Don't nap after 3pm.
- **Before races:** Sleep 2 nights before matters most (adrenaline disrupts night-before sleep).
- **After night races/events:** Extend sleep the following 2 nights by 1-2 hours.

### Sleep Hygiene for Runners
- No screens 60 min before bed (blue light suppresses melatonin)
- Room temp: 18-20°C (cool promotes deep sleep)
- No caffeine after 2pm (half-life: 5-6 hours)
- If training in evening: finish 3+ hours before bed
- Post-evening-run: cool shower to drop core temperature faster

## Active Recovery

### What It Is
Very low intensity movement (RPE 2-3) that promotes blood flow without adding training stress. Walking, cycling at conversational pace, swimming easy, yoga.

### When to Use
- Day after a hard session (intervals, tempo, long run)
- Between double-session days (elite)
- During deload weeks (replace one easy run with active recovery)

### What It Is NOT
- A recovery run at "just a little slower than easy" — that's still training stress
- Hot yoga or power yoga — that's another training stimulus
- "Light" intervals — no such thing for recovery purposes

### Evidence
Active recovery at 30-40% VO2max accelerates lactate clearance and reduces DOMS (delayed onset muscle soreness) vs complete rest. But: more is not better. 20-30 minutes is sufficient. Extended active recovery becomes training.

## Foam Rolling & Self-Massage

### What the Evidence Says
Foam rolling reduces DOMS perception by 10-20% and improves short-term range of motion (5-10 degrees). It does NOT break up scar tissue, realign fascia, or "release" muscles. The mechanism is likely neurological (pain gate theory + parasympathetic activation).

### Practical Guidelines
- Roll BEFORE runs for mobility (30-60s per area)
- Roll AFTER runs for recovery (60-90s per area)
- Moderate pressure — not excruciating. Pain tolerance doesn't equal effectiveness.
- Focus areas for runners: calves, quads, glutes, TFL/hip flexors, thoracic spine
- Do NOT foam roll: IT band directly (roll above/below), acute injuries, bones, lower back

### What Works Better
- Sleep (see above)
- Adequate nutrition (protein + carbs post-run)
- Easy movement (walking)
- Compression garments (modest evidence for reduced DOMS)

## Cold Water Immersion (Ice Baths)

### The Science
Cold exposure (10-15°C for 10-15 min) reduces inflammation and perceived soreness. HOWEVER: it also blunts the adaptive response to training (reduces muscle protein synthesis and mitochondrial biogenesis).

### When to Use
- Between competitions (multi-day events, tournament racing)
- After exceptionally hard sessions where next-day performance matters more than long-term adaptation
- During taper phase (adaptation already achieved, focus shifts to freshness)

### When NOT to Use
- During training blocks where you WANT adaptation
- After strength training (blunts hypertrophy)
- As a daily routine during base building

### Practical Protocol
- 10-15°C water (cold but not ice-solid)
- 10-15 minutes immersion (lower body minimum)
- Within 30 min of finishing the session
- Don't combine with hot-cold contrast (evidence is mixed/weak)

## Deload Weeks

### Purpose
Accumulated fatigue masks fitness. A deload allows fatigue to dissipate while fitness is maintained, revealing your actual performance level. It's NOT a sign of weakness — it's programmed supercompensation.

### Frequency
- Beginners: Every 4th week (3 hard : 1 easy)
- Intermediate: Every 3rd-4th week
- Advanced: Every 3rd week during intense blocks
- Listen to body: if 3+ of these present: elevated resting HR, poor sleep, declining motivation, persistent soreness, stalled performance → deload NOW regardless of schedule

### How to Deload
- Reduce VOLUME by 40-60% (fewer km, shorter long run)
- Maintain INTENSITY (keep one quality session but shorter)
- Keep FREQUENCY (same number of running days, just shorter)
- Maintain strength training (reduce sets, not exercises)
- Sleep more, eat well, don't cut calories during deload

### What NOT to Do During Deload
- "Make up for lost training" by adding extra sessions
- Panic about lost fitness (fitness takes 2-3 weeks to decline meaningfully)
- Sit completely still (some activity promotes recovery better than total rest)
- Diet or cut calories (your body needs fuel to repair)

## Recovery Timeline by Session Type

### Easy Run
Recovery: 12-24 hours. Can run again next day.

### Tempo/Threshold
Recovery: 24-48 hours. One easy day between tempos.

### VO2max Intervals
Recovery: 48-72 hours. Two easy days minimum between interval sessions.

### Long Run (>90 min)
Recovery: 48-72 hours. No quality sessions for 2-3 days after.

### Race (5K-10K)
Recovery: 3-5 days easy. No quality for 1 week.

### Race (Half Marathon)
Recovery: 7-10 days easy. No quality for 2 weeks.

### Race (Marathon)
Recovery: 2-4 weeks easy. No quality for 3-4 weeks. The muscle damage from a marathon takes 4-6 weeks to fully repair regardless of how you feel.

## Monitoring Recovery

### Resting Heart Rate
Measure upon waking (before getting up). Elevated 5+ bpm above personal baseline = under-recovered. Don't train hard.

### Heart Rate Variability (HRV)
Higher HRV = better recovered (more parasympathetic tone). Trending downward over 3+ days = accumulated fatigue. Requires consistent measurement (same time, same position, same app).

### Subjective Markers
Rate daily on 1-10:
- Sleep quality
- Muscle soreness
- Energy/motivation
- Mood

If 3+ markers are below 5 for 2+ consecutive days: reduce training or rest.

### The 10-Minute Test
Start running. If you feel better after 10 minutes than when you started: continue. If you feel the same or worse after 10 minutes: stop and call it a rest day. Trust the signal.
