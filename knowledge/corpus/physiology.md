# Running Physiology & Energy Systems

## VO2max — Maximum Oxygen Uptake

### What It Is
VO2max is the maximum rate at which your body can consume oxygen during intense exercise. Measured in ml/kg/min. It represents the ceiling of your aerobic engine. Elite male runners: 70-85 ml/kg/min. Elite female: 60-75. Recreational: 35-50.

### How It Improves
VO2max responds to training through increased cardiac output (stroke volume), improved capillary density in muscles, and greater mitochondrial density. Beginners see rapid gains (10-20% in first 3 months). Advanced runners improve 1-3% per year. Genetics set the ceiling; training determines how close you get.

### Training VO2max
Intervals at 95-100% max HR for 3-5 minutes with equal recovery. Classic session: 5x1000m at 3K-5K pace. Frequency: 1-2x per week during build phase. Too much VO2max work without aerobic base causes burnout.

## Lactate Threshold

### The Physiology
At rest, blood lactate is ~1 mmol/L. As intensity increases, lactate production exceeds clearance. Lactate threshold (LT1): ~2 mmol/L — the first rise above baseline. Onset of Blood Lactate Accumulation (OBLA/LT2): ~4 mmol/L — the point where lactate accumulates exponentially. Marathon pace sits near LT1. Tempo/threshold pace sits near LT2.

### Why It Matters
LT2 is the strongest predictor of endurance performance. A runner with moderate VO2max but high LT2 (as % of VO2max) outperforms one with high VO2max but low LT2. Elite marathoners run at 85-90% of VO2max at LT2.

### Training Lactate Threshold
Tempo runs at LT2 pace (comfortably hard, 20-40 min continuous). Cruise intervals: 3x10min at threshold with 1 min rest. Norwegian double-threshold: 2 threshold sessions per day, 4-5 days/week (elite only). Threshold improves with consistent work over months.

## Cardiac Adaptations

### Stroke Volume
Training increases left ventricle size and contractility. Each heartbeat pumps more blood. This is why resting HR decreases with fitness (fewer beats needed to supply same blood flow). Elite runners: resting HR 35-45 bpm.

### Cardiac Output
Cardiac output = stroke volume x heart rate. Maximum cardiac output determines VO2max ceiling. Untrained: ~20 L/min. Elite endurance: 35-40 L/min.

### Eccentric Cardiac Hypertrophy
Endurance training causes the heart walls to stretch (volume overload), increasing chamber size without wall thickening. This is "athlete's heart" — healthy, reversible, and performance-enhancing.

## Muscle Fiber Types

### Type I (Slow-Twitch)
Fatigue-resistant, high mitochondrial density, primary for aerobic running. Recruited first. Dominant in distance runners. Cannot produce high force/speed.

### Type IIa (Fast-Twitch Oxidative)
Moderate fatigue resistance. Recruited for tempo and threshold efforts. Can be trained to behave more like Type I through endurance training (fiber-type shift).

### Type IIx (Fast-Twitch Glycolytic)
High force, rapid fatigue. Recruited for sprints and very fast intervals. Important for kick finish in races but not primary for distance.

### Practical Implication
Distance training converts IIx → IIa → more I-like behavior. This is why marathon runners lose sprint speed. Maintaining some fast running (strides, short intervals) preserves neuromuscular recruitment patterns.

## Energy Systems

### Phosphocreatine (0-10 seconds)
Immediate energy. Sprint starts. Depleted in seconds. Irrelevant for distance running except race finish kicks.

### Anaerobic Glycolysis (10 seconds - 2 minutes)
Glucose → lactate. High power but limited duration. Dominant in 400-800m. Produces the burn. Not primary for distance but recruited during surges.

### Aerobic System (2+ minutes)
Oxygen + fuel (glycogen/fat) → ATP + CO2 + water. Dominant for all running beyond 800m. Glycogen stores: ~90 minutes at moderate intensity. Fat stores: virtually unlimited but slower to metabolize.

### The Crossover Concept
At low intensity: fat is primary fuel. As intensity increases, carbohydrate contribution rises. At threshold: ~70% carbs, 30% fat. At VO2max: ~95% carbs. Training the aerobic system improves fat oxidation at higher intensities (spares glycogen).

## Glycogen and the Marathon Wall

### Glycogen Storage
Muscles store ~400-500g glycogen. Liver stores ~100g. Total: ~2000-2500 calories. Marathon at moderate pace burns ~2600-3000 calories. The math doesn't work without fueling.

### Hitting the Wall
When glycogen depletes, pace drops 1-2 min/km. Fat oxidation cannot sustain marathon pace. This is the "wall" at 30-35km. Prevention: carb loading before, fueling during (60-90g carbs/hour for >90 min efforts), and training at lower intensities to improve fat oxidation.

### Carb Loading
3 days before race: increase carbs to 8-12g/kg/day while tapering volume. This supercompensates glycogen stores. Well-executed loading adds 20-30 minutes of glycogen availability.

## Thermoregulation

### Heat Production
Running produces 15-20x resting metabolic heat. Core temperature rises 1°C per 5-8 minutes of hard running without cooling. Sweat evaporation is the primary cooling mechanism.

### Performance Impact
For every 1°C rise in core temp above 38°C, VO2max decreases ~1.5%. Above 39.5°C: heat exhaustion risk. Above 40°C: heat stroke danger. Hot conditions reduce marathon performance by 2-10%.

### Heat Acclimatization
10-14 days of training in heat. Adaptations: earlier sweating, increased sweat rate, lower core temp at same pace, expanded plasma volume, reduced heart rate. Begin 2 weeks before hot-weather race.

## Altitude Physiology

### The Challenge
At altitude, reduced partial pressure of oxygen means less O2 delivery to muscles. VO2max decreases ~7% per 1000m above 1500m. Performance above threshold is disproportionately affected.

### Adaptation
2-4 weeks at altitude stimulates EPO production → increased red blood cells → better O2 carrying capacity. Benefits persist 2-3 weeks after return to sea level. "Live high, train low" maximizes adaptation while maintaining training intensity.

### Practical Guidelines
Race at altitude after 2+ weeks acclimatization or within 24 hours of arrival (before acute mountain sickness sets in). Reduce intensity expectations by 5-10% until acclimatized.

## Age-Related Decline

### The Science
VO2max declines ~1% per year after age 25 (0.5% with continued training). LT2 as % of VO2max can be maintained into the 60s with proper training. Running economy may actually improve with decades of practice.

### Practical Impact
A 40-year-old with 15 years of running can outperform their 25-year-old untrained self. Age grading tables show expected decline: a 50-year-old male running 20:00 for 5K is equivalent to a 25-year-old running ~17:00.

### Training Adjustments
More recovery between hard sessions (48-72h vs 24-48h for younger). Reduce plyometric volume. Prioritize mobility and strength. Maintain intensity but reduce frequency of quality sessions.
