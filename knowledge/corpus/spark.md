# Spark Coach — Beginner Methodology

## Philosophy
The Spark coach exists to transform non-runners into runners. The primary goal is CONSISTENCY, not speed. A beginner who runs 3x per week for 6 months will outperform one who trains hard for 3 weeks and quits. Habit formation is the product; fitness is the byproduct.

## Walk/Run Progressions

### The Run-Walk Method (Jeff Galloway)
Alternating running and walking intervals prevents overuse injury and makes running accessible. The walk breaks are strategic recovery, not failure.

### 8-Week Couch to 5K Progression
- Week 1: 1 min run / 2 min walk x 8 (24 min total)
- Week 2: 2 min run / 2 min walk x 6 (24 min total)
- Week 3: 3 min run / 1.5 min walk x 6 (27 min total)
- Week 4: 4 min run / 1 min walk x 5 (25 min total)
- Week 5: 5 min run / 1 min walk x 4 (24 min total)
- Week 6: 8 min run / 1 min walk x 3 (27 min total)
- Week 7: 12 min run / 1 min walk x 2 (26 min total)
- Week 8: 20-25 min continuous running

### When to Progress
Only advance to the next week when the current week feels comfortable on ALL three sessions. There is no shame in repeating a week. Progression signals: completing intervals without excessive breathlessness, recovering during walk breaks, finishing feeling like you could do one more interval.

### When NOT to Progress
- Still heavily breathing at end of run intervals
- Dreading the next session
- Any joint pain (not muscle soreness)
- Incomplete recovery between sessions (still fatigued 24h later)

## Habit Building Psychology

### Identity-Based Habits (James Clear)
Don't focus on "I want to run a 5K." Focus on "I am a runner." Every run, no matter how short, is a vote for your new identity. 3 runs of 15 minutes counts more than 1 run of 45 minutes for habit formation.

### The Two-Minute Rule
On days you don't want to run: commit to 2 minutes only. Put on shoes, walk outside, jog for 2 minutes. 80% of the time you'll continue. The other 20%, you still maintained the habit streak.

### Streak Mechanics
Track consecutive days with ANY running activity. Visual streak calendars create powerful commitment. Missing one day doesn't break the streak; missing two does. After a miss: the only goal is showing up the next scheduled day.

### Tiny Habits (BJ Fogg)
Anchor running to an existing habit. "After I have my morning coffee, I put on my running shoes." Start ridiculously small and grow. Celebration after completion ("I'm awesome") wires the neural reward.

### Consistency Over Intensity
3 easy runs per week for 6 months > 6 hard runs per week for 3 weeks. New runners should NEVER increase both frequency and intensity simultaneously. Add frequency first (2x -> 3x per week), then duration (20 min -> 30 min), then intensity last (easy -> some harder efforts).

## First 5K Race Guide

### 4 Weeks Before
Run 3x per week. Longest run should reach 4-4.5 km by race week. All runs at conversational pace. Don't try to "get fast" before your first race.

### Race Week
Reduce volume by 30%. No new shoes, foods, or gear on race day. Lay out everything the night before. Plan transportation and arrival (45 min before start).

### Pacing Strategy
Start SLOWER than you think. Walk the first 30 seconds if needed to avoid the adrenaline surge. Target negative splits (second half slightly faster than first). Walking is allowed and common in first races.

### During the Race
Focus on effort, not pace. If you can't speak a short sentence, slow down. Save energy for the final kilometer. High-five volunteers. Smile at photographers. This is a celebration.

### After the Race
Walk for 10-15 minutes. Hydrate. Eat within 60 minutes. Take 3-4 days easy/off. Reflect on the achievement: you are now someone who has RACED. That's a permanent identity shift.

## Common Beginner Injuries

### Shin Splints (Medial Tibial Stress Syndrome)
Pain along the inner shin bone. Caused by: too much too soon, running on hard surfaces, worn shoes, weak calves. Treatment: reduce volume 50%, ice after runs, calf raises (3x15 daily), foam roll. Prevention: gradual progression, proper shoes, strength work.

### Runner's Knee (Patellofemoral Pain)
Dull ache around or behind kneecap. Caused by: weak quads/glutes, overpronation, sudden volume increase. Treatment: reduce volume, strengthen VMO (quad) and glutes, avoid downhill running. Prevention: single-leg squats, hip bridges, gradual progression.

### Plantar Fasciitis
Sharp heel pain, worst in first morning steps. Caused by: tight calves, weak foot muscles, sudden volume increase, being overweight. Treatment: roll foot on frozen water bottle, calf stretches, toe curls. Prevention: gradual progression, calf raises, proper arch support.

## Breathing for New Runners

### Rhythmic Breathing
Match breathing to footsteps. Beginners: inhale for 3 steps, exhale for 2 steps (3:2 pattern). This creates a 5-step cycle that alternates which foot is landing on the exhale, reducing asymmetric stress.

### Nose vs Mouth Breathing
Both are fine. At easy pace, nose breathing may be possible and encourages truly easy effort. At any harder effort, mouth breathing is necessary and normal. Don't force nose-only if it causes distress.

### The Talk Test
The simplest intensity monitor for beginners. If you can speak a complete sentence (not just a word), you're at the right pace. If you can only gasp single words, slow down. If you can sing, you could go slightly faster.

## When to Progress from Walk/Run to Continuous Running

### Ready Signs
- Completing 8-10 min run intervals comfortably
- Running 3x per week for at least 4 weeks
- No pain during or after runs
- Heart rate returning to below 120 bpm within 2 min of walking
- WANTING to run longer (not dreading it)

### How to Transition
Don't suddenly jump to 30 min continuous. Gradually reduce walk intervals: 8 run/30s walk -> 10 run/30s walk -> 12 run/30s walk -> 15 min continuous -> 20 min -> 25 min -> 30 min. Each jump: 2 weeks minimum.

## Why Easy Pace Feels Too Slow

New runners are often frustrated that "easy pace" feels embarrassingly slow. Explanation: your aerobic system is underdeveloped. Your legs can run faster, but your heart, lungs, and capillaries cannot support that pace without going anaerobic. Running easy builds the aerobic engine that makes ALL paces eventually faster. The slow miles make the fast miles possible. Every elite runner in the world runs most miles slowly.

## Building to 3x Per Week

### The First Month
- Week 1: 2 sessions (Tue/Sat or Mon/Thu)
- Week 2: 2 sessions
- Week 3: 3 sessions (Mon/Wed/Sat)
- Week 4: 3 sessions

Never run consecutive days in the first 3 months. Rest days are when adaptation happens. If anything hurts, take an extra rest day without guilt. The goal is to still be running in 6 months.

## Spark Coach Guardrails (Hard Rules)

- NEVER prescribe intervals, tempo runs, or hill sprints to a Spark runner
- NEVER recommend running through pain — always rest first, reassess after 48h
- Maximum volume: 15km per week
- Maximum duration: 40 minutes per session
- Minimum rest days: 3 per week
- NEVER shame a runner for walking or taking rest
- ALWAYS celebrate consistency over performance
- If a runner reports sharp pain: STOP immediately, recommend medical assessment
- No fasted running for beginners (blood sugar crashes risk)


## Spark Mentality - Coaching the Beginner
The beginner's enemy is not fitness; it is quitting. Every coaching decision is filtered through one question: "Will this make them more likely to run again tomorrow?" Protect the fragile new habit above all. Lower every barrier. Make success feel inevitable. The beginner does not need a hard workout - they need a streak. Your tone is warm, patient, and relentlessly encouraging. Never shame a missed run; celebrate the return.

## Spark Psychology - Building the Runner Identity
A new runner is mid-transformation from "person who doesn't run" to "runner." This identity is delicate. Reinforce it constantly: "That's what runners do." Use tiny wins to stack evidence - a completed 10-minute jog is a victory worth naming. Combat all-or-nothing thinking ("I only ran twice this week" becomes "You ran twice this week - that's two more than zero, and it counts"). The two-day rule is sacred here: never miss twice.

### Common Beginner Fears
Fear of judgment ("everyone is watching me"), fear of pain, fear of failure, and comparison to faster runners. Address each directly: nobody is watching; discomfort is normal and fades; there is no failing at building a habit; comparison is the thief of joy - the only comparison that matters is to last week's self.

## Spark Tips and Tactics
- Run-walk is a strategy, not a weakness - even experienced runners use it.
- Talk test: if you can't speak in sentences, slow down or walk. Easy is the goal.
- Lay out clothes the night before to remove morning friction.
- The first 10 minutes are the hardest - promise yourself only 10, and usually you'll continue.
- Track consistency (days run), not pace or distance. Pace will come on its own.
- New shoes from a running store and a gradual start prevent most early injuries.

## Spark Planning Mode
Keep it dead simple: 3 non-consecutive days per week, each a short run-walk, with full rest between. Progress by adding a minute of running to intervals, never by adding hard efforts. A Couch-to-5K style 8-12 week progression is ideal. No tempo, no intervals, no hills, no racing yet. The plan for a beginner is a calendar of small, achievable, repeatable wins - the goal is 6 months of consistency, after which fitness takes care of itself.
