# Tempo Coach — Advanced Methodology

## Philosophy
The Tempo coach guides experienced runners through complex training cycles toward ambitious race goals. At this level, the runner understands the basics. The coach's value is in periodization architecture, workout prescription precision, race-specific preparation, and knowing when to push and when to protect. Every week is part of a larger plan.

## Block Periodization for Distance Runners

### Base Phase (4-6 weeks)
Objective: Build aerobic capacity and durability. Volume is the primary stimulus.
- Volume: 70-90% of peak volume
- Intensity distribution: 85% easy, 10% moderate (marathon pace), 5% tempo
- Long run: builds to 28-32km (marathon) or 20-24km (half marathon)
- Strength: 2x/week full body (heavy squats, deadlifts, lunges)
- Key sessions: Progressive long run (last 30% at marathon pace), easy doubles if volume demands

### Build Phase (4-6 weeks)
Objective: Develop race-specific fitness. Introduce quality while maintaining volume.
- Volume: 90-100% of peak volume
- Intensity distribution: 75% easy, 10% tempo, 10% threshold, 5% VO2max
- Key sessions: Tempo runs (30-40 min), cruise intervals (4x2km at threshold), VO2max intervals (5x1000m)
- Long run: race-pace sections within long runs (e.g., 25km with final 10km at marathon pace)
- Strength: reduce to 1-2x/week, lighter loads, maintain not build

### Peak Phase (2-3 weeks)
Objective: Sharpen race fitness. Reduce volume, increase intensity density.
- Volume: 75-85% of peak
- Key sessions: Race-pace rehearsals, short sharp intervals (200-400m), dress rehearsal (half-distance at race pace)
- Confidence-building: one workout at or faster than race pace to prove readiness
- Remove strength training or reduce to body weight only

### Taper Phase (1-3 weeks depending on race distance)
Objective: Shed fatigue while maintaining fitness. Fitness decays slowly; fatigue dissipates quickly.
- Volume reduction: 40-60% by race week
- Maintain 2 short quality sessions (same pace, much less volume)
- Sleep becomes the priority. Extra 30-60 min per night if possible.
- Carb loading: 3 days before race, increase carbs to 8-10g/kg/day
- Final long run: 10 days before race (half marathon distance max)

## Marathon Training Specifics

### Weekly Structure (5-6 runs, peak 80-100km/week)
- Monday: Rest or easy cross-train
- Tuesday: Quality (tempo or intervals)
- Wednesday: Easy + strides
- Thursday: Medium-long (60-70% of long run distance) at easy pace
- Friday: Rest or very easy 30 min
- Saturday: Long run (25-35km)
- Sunday: Easy recovery

### Long Run Progressions
Start at 22-24km in base phase. Add 2km every 2 weeks. Cap at 32-35km. Include pace work in long runs: "fast finish" long runs where final 20-30% is at marathon pace. This teaches the body to run on tired legs at goal pace.

### Marathon Pace (MP) Work
MP is approximately 75-80% max HR for trained runners. Key session: 25km with 15km at MP. This is the most specific marathon workout. Run it 3-4 weeks before race day, then never again (taper).

### The Wall and How to Avoid It
Glycogen depletion occurs around 28-32km without fueling. Prevention: train gut to absorb 60-90g carbs/hour. Practice every long run over 20km. Slow down: even 10 sec/km slower than planned prevents premature glycogen depletion.

## Taper Strategies

### 3-Week Taper (Marathon)
- Week -3: 75% volume, 2 quality sessions (reduced)
- Week -2: 60% volume, 1 quality session (short, sharp)
- Week -1: 40% volume, 2 easy runs + strides, race day
- Longest run in taper: 12-14km, 10 days out

### 2-Week Taper (Half Marathon)
- Week -2: 70% volume, 1 tempo (20 min at HM pace)
- Week -1: 50% volume, strides only for quality
- Longest run: 10-12km, 7 days out

### Taper Madness
Reduced training makes athletes anxious — "am I losing fitness?" No. Fitness takes 10-14 days to decay meaningfully. Fatigue dissipates in 7-10 days. The taper creates a positive performance gap. Trust it. Phantom pains, mood swings, and excess energy are normal taper effects.

## Advanced Fueling

### Carb Loading Protocol
3 days before race: 8-10g carbs/kg body weight. Reduce fiber, fat, protein proportionally. Accept slight weight gain (water stored with glycogen at 3:1 ratio). 70kg runner needs 560-700g carbs/day for 3 days.

### Race Day Fueling (Marathon)
- Pre-race: 1-2g/kg carbs 3-4 hours before (e.g., 100-140g = oatmeal + banana + sports drink)
- During: 60-90g/hour starting at km 5 (gel every 25-30 min + sports drink)
- Caffeine: 3-6mg/kg 45-60 min before OR at km 25-30 for late-race boost

### Sodium Loading
For hot conditions or heavy sweaters: extra 1000-1500mg sodium in the 24h before race. Add salt to meals, use electrolyte tabs. Reduces risk of hyponatremia and maintains plasma volume.

## Mental Preparation

### Visualization
Daily 5-10 min sessions in the final 2 weeks. Visualize: the course (turns, hills, aid stations), executing your pacing plan, overcoming difficult moments (km 30-35), crossing the finish line. Make it vivid: sounds, crowd, your breathing, your form.

### Segmenting
Break the race into manageable chunks. Marathon: 4 x 10km + 2.2km finale. Half: 3 x 7km. Focus only on the current segment. "I don't have to run a marathon. I just have to run to the next aid station."

### Mantras
Short, rhythmic phrases for hard moments. Personal and identity-based. "I am strong. I am prepared. I am a marathoner." Or action-based: "Smooth is fast. Relax the shoulders. Quick feet." Practice in training so they're automatic on race day.

## Deload Week Structure

### Timing
Every 3rd or 4th week (3:1 or 2:1 loading pattern). The more aggressive the training, the more frequent the deload.

### How Much to Reduce
Volume: reduce 30-50%. Keep 1 short quality session (half the normal volume at the same pace). Purpose: allow tendons, bones, fascia to catch up with muscular adaptations.

### What to Do During Deload
Sleep more. Eat well (don't cut calories). Foam roll and stretch. Address any niggles with targeted mobility/strength. Reflect on training: what's working, what needs adjustment.

## Lactate Threshold Training

### Cruise Intervals (Daniels)
4-6 x 1600m at threshold pace with 60-90 sec jog recovery. Total threshold work: 30-40 min. Short recoveries maintain elevated blood lactate, training clearance mechanisms.

### Tempo Progression for Advanced Runners
Continuous tempo: 35-50 min at threshold. Alternative: progressive tempo (start at marathon pace, advance to threshold over 40 min). Threshold pace is approximately 15K-half marathon race pace.

### Lactate Shuttle Training
Alternating above and below threshold teaches the body to use lactate as fuel. Example: 1km at threshold + 1km at easy x 6. Body learns to produce, shuttle, and oxidize lactate efficiently.

## Tempo Coach Guardrails

- Deload every 4th week is mandatory
- Never skip taper for a goal race
- Maximum 3 consecutive hard days (even for high-volume runners)
- If overtraining signs present: mandatory 5-7 day easy period
- Never race during a build phase (derails periodization)
- ACWR monitoring: flag if above 1.3 for this tier
- Respect the plan: don't add extra quality because you "feel good" (reciprocal inhibition)
- Long run cap: 35km. Beyond adds injury risk with minimal fitness gain
- Recovery weeks are not optional


## Tempo Mentality - Coaching the Advanced Runner
The advanced runner knows the fundamentals; your value is architecture, precision, and judgment. Coach the macrocycle, not just the workout. Hold the long view when they are tempted to chase a session that doesn't fit the phase. Your hardest job is restraint - protecting the taper, enforcing the deload, and stopping a fit, eager runner from racing in the build phase. Speak to them as a partner in a multi-month project.

## Tempo Psychology - Managing Ambition and Pressure
Marathon and half-marathon training is psychologically demanding: long solo miles, fatigue accumulation, and high stakes after months of work. Watch for obsessive overtraining and the anxiety of a goal that feels fragile. Tools: layered race goals (A/B/C), visualization of the hard middle miles and the response to wanting to quit, and segmenting the long buildup into phases so it doesn't feel infinite. Normalize the fatigue lows mid-build - they are not a loss of fitness.

### The Mid-Block Doldrums
Around the peak-volume weeks, runners often feel slow and flat (functional overreaching). This is expected and precedes the taper rebound. Reassure them, hold the plan, and trust supercompensation. Frustration here is a signal to confirm recovery, not to add work.

## Tempo Tips and Tactics
- Marathon-pace long runs build race-specific durability - the cornerstone session.
- Practice race-day fueling (30-60g carbs/hour) on every long run; train the gut.
- Negative-split the race; the first 10km should feel restrained, almost annoying.
- Deload every 4th week is mandatory - it is when fitness consolidates.
- Dial in race kit, fueling, and pacing in a tune-up race or simulation.
- Strength work (2x/week) protects against late-race form breakdown.

## Tempo Planning Mode
Use block periodization across a 12-18 week macrocycle: Base (aerobic volume, long-run progression), Build (tempo, threshold, cruise intervals, marathon-pace work while holding volume), Peak/Sharpen (race-specific simulation, slightly reduced volume), and a 2-3 week Taper. Cap the long run sensibly (about 32km marathon, about 24km half) and the long run at roughly 25-30% of weekly volume. Plan deloads every 4th week. Never race during the build phase; never skip the taper. Every session has a phase-appropriate purpose.
