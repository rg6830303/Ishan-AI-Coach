# Apex Coach — Elite Methodology

## Philosophy
The Apex coach manages athletes at the edge of their potential. At this level, marginal gains matter. The difference between a PR and a plateau is often 2-3% — found in recovery optimization, load management precision, and race-specific peaking. The Apex coach uses data (HRV, ACWR, strain) to make daily training decisions. Intuition is validated by numbers.

## HRV-Guided Training Load Management

### What HRV Tells Us
Heart Rate Variability (RMSSD or LnRMSSD) reflects autonomic nervous system balance. High HRV = parasympathetic dominance = recovered and ready. Low HRV = sympathetic dominance = stressed, fatigued, or sick. The trend matters more than absolute values.

### Daily Decision Protocol
- HRV within normal range (individual 7-day rolling average +/- 0.5 CV): train as planned
- HRV below normal by 1+ SD: reduce intensity to easy only, or rest
- HRV below normal 3+ consecutive days: mandatory easy/off for 2-3 days, investigate (sleep, stress, illness)
- HRV elevated above normal: peak readiness day — ideal for quality session or time trial

### Using HRV for Periodization
Track HRV across training blocks. During base phase: HRV should trend upward (growing aerobic fitness). During build/peak: HRV drops slightly (functional overreaching). During taper: HRV rebounds above pre-training levels = supercompensation = RACE.

### Common Mistakes
- Reacting to single-day HRV drops (normal variance)
- Ignoring sustained HRV decline (3+ days)
- Not controlling measurement conditions (measure at same time, same position, same duration daily)
- Using HRV to justify going harder (HRV high = ready for quality, NOT ready for maximum)

## ACWR (Acute:Chronic Workload Ratio) Management

### The Concept
Acute workload = training load of the current week. Chronic workload = rolling 4-week average. ACWR = acute / chronic. Sweet spot: 0.8 - 1.3. Risk zone: > 1.5. Critical: > 1.8.

### Calculating Training Load
TSS (Training Stress Score) = duration x intensity factor. Or simpler: TRIMP = duration (min) x average HR fraction. Sum weekly TRIMP for acute load.

### Application
- Building volume: increase ACWR to 1.1-1.2 per week maximum
- After illness/injury: ACWR will spike if you return too fast. Resume at 50% and rebuild over 2 weeks
- Pre-race: ACWR drops during taper (0.6-0.8). This is expected and desired.
- Danger signal: ACWR > 1.5 for 2+ consecutive weeks = injury risk multiplied 2-4x

### Workload Management Strategy
Plan weekly TSS targets in advance. If you miss a day, do NOT make it up by cramming into fewer days. Accept the lower volume. Consistency of load distribution matters more than hitting exact weekly totals.

## Peaking Protocols

### Single Peak (One A-Race Per Season)
16-20 week cycle: 8 weeks base -> 6 weeks build -> 2 weeks sharpen -> 2 weeks taper -> RACE. All other races are training races (B-priority, run at 90% effort without tapering).

### Double Peak (Two A-Races)
Typically 12-16 weeks apart. First peak: full taper, race, 1 week recovery. Bridge: 3-4 weeks moderate training to re-establish load. Second buildup: 4-6 weeks. Second peak: full taper, race.

### Signs of Peak Readiness
- HRV returning to or exceeding pre-training baseline
- Resting HR at lowest point of the cycle
- "Bouncy legs" feeling on easy runs
- Sudden improvement in sleep quality
- Race-pace efforts feeling easier than expected in rehearsal workouts
- Restlessness and excess energy on rest days

## Race-Specific Training Blocks

### 5K-Specific (4-Week Sharpening Block)
Key sessions:
- 12 x 400m at 5K pace, 60s recovery
- 6 x 1000m at 5K pace, 2 min recovery
- 2 x 2000m + 2 x 1000m at 5K pace, 2 min recovery
- Time trial: 3000m at 3-5 sec/km faster than 5K goal
Volume: 80% of peak. Quality: 2 sessions per week. Easy: truly easy.

### Marathon-Specific (6-Week Block)
Key sessions:
- 30km with final 15km at marathon pace
- 3 x 5km at marathon pace, 1km easy between
- 20km at marathon pace (dress rehearsal, 4 weeks out)
- 16km progressive: 8km easy -> 4km MP -> 4km half-marathon pace
Volume: 90-100% peak. Long run: marathon-specific every other week.

### Half-Marathon Specific (4-Week Block)
Key sessions:
- 6 x 2km at half-marathon pace, 90s recovery
- 16km with final 10km at HM pace
- 10km at HM pace continuous (confidence builder)
- 5 x 1km at 10K pace (sharper than race pace to build speed reserve)

## Taper Optimization by Distance

### 5K Taper (7-10 days)
- Day -10: Last hard long interval session (reduced volume)
- Days -9 to -4: Easy runs, gradually reducing volume
- Day -3: Short sharpener (4 x 200m at mile pace)
- Day -2: 20 min easy + 4 strides
- Day -1: Rest or 10 min jog
Total volume in taper week: 50% of peak week.

### 10K Taper (10-14 days)
- Day -14: Last full quality session
- Days -13 to -8: Easy runs, volume at 70%
- Day -7: Short tempo (15 min at 10K pace)
- Days -6 to -3: Easy, reducing to 40%
- Day -2: 15 min easy + strides
- Day -1: Rest

### Marathon Taper (3 weeks)
See Tempo coach methodology. Apex athletes: may benefit from slightly more volume during taper (maintaining neural pathways) but never more intensity than 2 short sessions per week.

## Overreaching vs. Overtraining

### Functional Overreaching
Planned, short-term (1-2 weeks) period of high load. Performance initially declines, then supercompensates after adequate rest. The mechanism behind taper magic. Duration: max 2 weeks followed by 7-10 days recovery.

### Non-Functional Overreaching
Unplanned overload lasting 2-4 weeks. Performance stagnates or declines. Recovery requires 2-4 weeks of reduced training. Warning signs: persistent fatigue, sleep disruption, loss of motivation, frequent illness, elevated resting HR.

### Overtraining Syndrome (OTS)
Severe, requiring months of recovery. Hormonal disruption (cortisol chronically elevated, testosterone suppressed). Psychological symptoms: depression, apathy, loss of competitive drive. Prevention: never ignore 2+ weeks of declining performance + elevated resting HR + poor sleep.

### Detection Protocol
Daily monitoring: resting HR, HRV, sleep quality (1-10), energy (1-10), muscle soreness (1-10). Weekly check: if 3+ metrics trend negative for 7+ days = forced deload regardless of training plan.

## Strain Ceilings and Weekly TSS Budgets

### Concept
Even elite athletes have a maximum weekly training load they can absorb and recover from. This is individual and discovered through careful experimentation over years.

### Establishing Your Ceiling
Start conservative. Track weekly TSS + next week's performance. If performance improves: current load is absorbable. If performance stagnates after 3+ weeks at a load: you've found your ceiling.

### Typical TSS Ranges (Running Only)
- Well-trained competitive: 400-600 TSS/week
- Elite amateur: 600-800 TSS/week
- Professional: 800-1200 TSS/week

Never exceed your established ceiling by more than 10% for more than 1 week. Chronic load above ceiling = non-functional overreaching.

## Advanced Periodization Systems

### Lydiard (Base-First)
Massive aerobic base (100+ miles/week for elites) followed by hill bounding, then track speed, then coordination (short sprints), then taper. Philosophy: you can never have too large an aerobic base.

### Daniels (VDOT-Based)
Training paces derived from recent race performance (VDOT). Four quality types: Easy, Marathon, Threshold, Interval, Repetition. Mesocycles rotate emphasis but maintain all qualities.

### Canova (Specific-to-General)
Controversial: begins with race-specific work, then adds general fitness. "The marathon doesn't require VO2max speed; it requires efficiency AT marathon pace." Marathon-specific long runs from week 1.

## Apex Coach Guardrails

- ACWR must remain below 1.5. Above 1.3 = caution flag
- HRV overrides the plan: sustained drops (3+ days) = mandatory easy
- Never ignore overtraining warning signs for a race
- Strain ceiling is individual and sacred
- Mandatory taper for all A-races (non-negotiable)
- No racing when ACWR > 1.3 (injury risk unacceptable)
- Rest at least 1 day per week (even at 100+ km/week)
- If illness: no running with fever, no hard running for 1 day per day of illness after recovery
- Medical scope: never diagnose, always refer for persistent or worsening symptoms
- Red flags: chest pain, dizziness, unusual breathlessness = STOP and seek medical attention immediately
