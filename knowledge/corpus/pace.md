# Pace Coach — Intermediate Methodology

## Philosophy
The Pace coach develops consistent runners into purposeful athletes. The key transition: from "just running" to TRAINING with intention. Every run has a purpose. Easy days are genuinely easy. Hard days are hard enough to stimulate adaptation. The intermediate runner learns to differentiate effort.

## The 10% Volume Rule

### The Rule
Never increase total weekly running volume (km or time) by more than 10% from the previous week. This is the single most important injury prevention tool for intermediate runners.

### Exceptions
- Returning from a deload: can jump back to previous peak (not a new high)
- Adding a 4th run day: new run should be short (3-5km easy), total volume may increase 15%
- Weather cancellation makeup: spread across remaining days, don't double up

### Application Example
Week 1: 25km -> Week 2: 27.5km -> Week 3: 30km -> Week 4 (deload): 20km -> Week 5: 30km (back to peak, not 22km)

## Easy/Tempo/Interval Mix for Intermediate Runners

### Weekly Structure (3-4 runs)
- 3 days/week: 2 easy + 1 quality (tempo OR intervals, not both)
- 4 days/week: 2 easy + 1 tempo + 1 easy/long
- 5 days/week: 3 easy + 1 tempo + 1 interval (only after 6+ months of consistent running)

### Quality Session Spacing
Minimum 48 hours between hard efforts. A Tuesday tempo means no Thursday intervals — do them Friday instead. The body needs 48-72 hours to absorb hard training and begin adaptation.

### Volume Distribution
Long run: 25-30% of weekly volume. Quality sessions: 15-20% combined. Easy runs: 50-60%. If weekly volume is 30km: long run 8-9km, quality session 4-6km hard work, easy runs 5-6km each.

## Race Pacing Strategy for 5K-10K

### 5K Pacing
Even splits or slight negative split (+2-5 sec per km for first km). First 400m slightly conservative (adrenaline management). Middle 3km: settle into race pace. Final 1km: empty the tank gradually.

### 10K Pacing
More conservative start than 5K. First 2km: 3-5 sec/km slower than goal pace. KM 3-8: lock into goal pace. KM 9-10: if energy allows, push to 5K effort for final 2km.

### Race Effort by Distance
5K effort = 93-97% max HR sustained. 10K effort = 88-92% max HR sustained. If you can't sustain the pace past halfway, you went out too fast. The most common racing mistake is too-fast first kilometer.

## Fueling for Runs Over 60 Minutes

### When to Start
Any run exceeding 60 minutes benefits from mid-run carbohydrate. Glycogen depletion causes "bonking" — the wall. Your body stores approximately 90 minutes of glycogen at tempo effort, less at higher intensities.

### What to Take
30-60g carbs per hour for runs 60-90 minutes. Options: sports drink (easy to consume), energy gels (concentrated, portable), dates/bananas (whole food alternative). Practice in training — never try new nutrition on race day.

### Gut Training
The gut adapts to processing fuel during exercise. Start with small amounts (15g/30min) and increase over weeks. Practice your exact race nutrition during long runs.

## Introduction to Speedwork

### Strides (Gateway Speed)
6-8 x 80-100m accelerations after easy runs. Build to 90-95% sprint over the first 50m, hold, decelerate. Full recovery (60-90 sec walk) between. Purpose: neuromuscular, running economy. Low fatigue cost, high return. 2-3x per week.

### Fartlek (Unstructured Speed)
"Speed play" in Swedish. During an easy run, include 6-10 surges of 30-90 seconds at approximately 5K-10K effort. Recovery: jog until breathing normalizes. Benefits: introduces intensity without the psychological pressure of track intervals. Time-based, not distance-based.

### Tempo Progression for Intermediates
- Month 1: 2 x 8 min at tempo with 2 min easy between
- Month 2: 2 x 10 min at tempo with 90 sec easy
- Month 3: 1 x 20 min continuous tempo
- Month 4: 1 x 25 min continuous tempo
- Month 5: 1 x 30 min continuous tempo

Tempo pace: approximately 10K race pace + 15-20 sec/km. Should feel "comfortably hard" — you can speak a few words but not sentences.

## Periodization for 10K Training (8-12 Week Block)

### Phase 1: Base (Weeks 1-4)
Focus: aerobic volume, consistency, easy running with strides. Volume builds 10% per week. Quality: strides only (no tempo/intervals). Long run extends to 12-15km by end of phase.

### Phase 2: Build (Weeks 5-8)
Focus: introduce quality, lactate threshold development. Volume holds or increases slightly. Add: 1 tempo session per week (progressive duration). Long run: maintain 13-16km, add final 2km at tempo.

### Phase 3: Sharpen (Weeks 9-11)
Focus: race-specific speed, 10K pace work. Volume decreases 10%. Add: 1 interval session (6 x 1000m at 10K pace, 2 min jog recovery). Tempo becomes race-pace rehearsal. Long run: reduce to 12km.

### Phase 4: Taper (Week 12)
Volume drops 40-50%. Maintain 1-2 short quality sessions (reduced volume, same intensity). Final long run: 8-10km, 10 days before race. Race day: well-rested, glycogen-loaded, confident.

## Recovery Between Quality Sessions

### The 48-Hour Rule
After any session at tempo effort or harder, allow minimum 48 hours before the next quality session. This means: Tuesday tempo -> Thursday intervals (OK). Tuesday tempo -> Wednesday intervals (NOT OK).

### Active Recovery Days
Easy runs on recovery days should be genuinely easy: RPE 3-4, conversational pace, shorter than normal. If an easy day feels hard, you're not recovered. Take the day off instead.

### Signs of Under-Recovery
- Resting HR elevated 5+ bpm above normal
- Motivation declining across multiple days
- Performance declining despite adequate sleep
- Persistent muscle soreness beyond 48 hours
- Irritability, disrupted sleep
When 2+ signs present: take 2-3 days completely off, then resume at 70% volume.

## When to Race vs. Train Through

### Race If:
- You've completed at least 8 weeks of consistent training
- Your longest run is at least 1.5x race distance (for 5K: 8km+ long run; for 10K: 15km+ long run)
- You're healthy (no nagging injuries)
- You have 2+ weeks before your next goal race
- The race fits into your training plan as a tempo effort or time trial

### Train Through If:
- The race is a B-race (not your priority)
- You're mid-build phase and don't want to taper
- Use it as a tempo workout at 80-90% race effort
- Don't peak for minor races — save that for your A-race

## Pace Coach Guardrails

- 10% rule is sacred — never exceed it
- 48 hours minimum between quality sessions
- Never recommend VO2max intervals over 1km for Pace runners
- No doubles (two runs per day)
- Maximum 4 quality sessions per month (not per week)
- If pain persists 2+ runs: reduce volume and reassess
- Always include warm-up (10 min easy) before quality sessions
- Cool-down: 5-10 min easy jog after hard efforts
- Weekly volume cap: 40km (until at this level consistently for 3+ months)
